## 1. What is NLP?

### Definition

**Natural Language Processing (NLP)** is a branch of Artificial Intelligence that sits at the intersection of computer science and linguistics. It has **two fundamental goals:**

|Goal|Description|
|---|---|
|**Science Goal**|Understand how human language works — its structure, meaning, and use|
|**Engineering Goal**|Build systems that can analyze and generate language; reduce the **man-machine gap**|

### The Two Pillars: NLU and NLG

$$\text{NLP} = \text{NLU} + \text{NLG}$$

- **NLU (Natural Language Understanding):** Takes **text as input**, produces **meaning** as output. Going from surface form to interpretation.
- **NLG (Natural Language Generation):** Takes **meaning/data as input**, produces **text** as output. Going from structured knowledge to human-readable language.

Think of it this way: when you read a sentence and understand it, that is NLU. When you write a sentence to express an idea, that is NLG.

## 2. NLP Pipeline — The Previous Generation

Before deep learning, NLP was handled through a layered pipeline of linguistic analysis, each layer building on the previous:

```
Text Input
    ↓
Phonetics / Phonology   (speech sounds)
    ↓
Morphology              (word structure)
    ↓
Lexical Analysis        (word meaning from dictionary)
    ↓
Syntactic Analysis      (sentence structure)
    ↓
Semantic Analysis       (meaning of the sentence)
    ↓
Pragmatics              (speaker intention, world knowledge)
    ↓
Discourse               (meaning across sentences)
    ↓
Understood Meaning
```

### 2.1 Phonetics and Phonology

Deals with the **processing of speech**. The key challenge is **ambiguity**:

- **Homophones** — words that sound identical but mean different things:
    - `bank` (financial institution) vs `bank` (river bank)
    - `alter` (to change) vs `altar` (church table)
    - `loan` vs `lone`

### 2.2 Morphology

Morphology studies how words are **formed from smaller units (morphemes)**. It helps disambiguate by rules of word formation:

- **Nouns:** Plural formation → boy → boys; Gender marking → czar → czarina
- **Verbs:** Tense → stretch → stretched

**Key insight:** Languages vary richly in morphology:

- **Rich morphology** (Dravidian, Hungarian, Turkish) — words carry a lot of grammatical information → easier higher-level processing
- **Poor morphology** (Chinese, English) — less inflection, more ambiguity at higher levels

A key task in CS: using **Finite State Machines** for word morphology.

### 2.3 Lexical Analysis

Uses a **lexicon (dictionary)** to look up word properties. For example, the word `dog` in a lexicon would contain:

- Part of speech: noun
- Morphological property: takes `'s'` in plural
- Semantic properties: animate, four-legged, carnivore

#### Lexical Disambiguation — Two Steps:

1. **Part-of-Speech Disambiguation:** Is "dog" a noun (animal) or verb (to pursue)?
2. **Sense Disambiguation:** Is "dog" the animal or "a very detestable person"?

> "Words have many meanings. We need to decide which meaning by understanding the **context**!"

#### Lexicons Evolve

Language is alive. New words and meanings emerge constantly:

- `justify` — now means "to align text margins" (word processing context)
- `Xeroxed` — became a verb meaning "to photocopy"
- `Helicopter parenting` — over-parenting

### 2.4 Syntactic Analysis

Studies the **grammatical structure** of sentences. Key insight: structure determines meaning.

"I like mangoes" has the parse tree:

```
      S
     / \
    NP  VP
    |   / \
    I   V   NP
       |    |
      like mangoes
```

#### Syntactic Ambiguity

The sentence `Mary saw the man on the mountain with a telescope` has **5 different meanings** depending on how the prepositional phrases attach:

- Mary used the telescope to see the man
- The man was on the mountain (and had a telescope)
- Mary was on the mountain with a telescope
- The mountain had a telescope
- …and so on

Even more complex: `Camera man shot the man with the gun when he was near Tendulkar` produces **2 × 2 × 2 = 8** possible meanings due to:

1. Two meanings of "shot" (photographed or fired at)
2. Two prepositional phrase attachments for "with the gun"
3. Ambiguity in who "he" refers to

**Tools to disambiguate syntax:**

- **Higher-level semantics:** "I saw the boy with a pony tail" — a ponytail cannot be an instrument of seeing, so it must describe the boy.
- **Pragmatics:** World knowledge tells us "(old men) and women" were taken to safety (not just "old [men and women]")
- **Preposition phrase attachment:** "I saw the mountain with the telescope" — mountains cannot hold telescopes, so the telescope belongs to the seer.

### 2.5 Semantic Analysis

**Semantic roles** identify who did what to whom. Example:

> "John gave a book to Mary"

- **Action:** give
- **Agent (doer):** John
- **Object:** book
- **Recipient:** Mary

Knowledge representation uses frameworks like:

- Predicate calculus
- Semantic Nets
- Frames
- Conceptual Dependencies and Scripts

**Challenge:** Semantic role labeling has ambiguity:

> "Visiting aunts can be a nuisance" — the aunt can be BOTH the agent (she is visiting) AND the object (you are visiting her).

### 2.6 Pragmatics

Pragmatics is the **hardest problem** in NLP. It requires:

1. **Modeling user intention:** The boy said "Yes sir, the sandals are there" when told to "go see if my sandals are under the divan." The _intended_ meaning was for him to bring them back!
    
2. **World knowledge:** A newspaper headline "WHY INDIA NEEDS A SECOND OCTOBER" requires knowing that October 2nd is Gandhi Jayanti — the context-dependent meaning is entirely unavailable from the words alone.
    

### 2.7 Discourse

Discourse is the **processing of sequences of sentences** where meaning builds across the text.

**Famous example:**

> "John was returning from school dejected — today was the math test. He couldn't control the class. The teacher shouldn't have made him responsible. After all, he is just a janitor."

We form hypotheses as we read:

- After sentence 1: John is a student
- After sentence 2: John might be the teacher
- After sentence 3: John might be the monitor
- After sentence 4: John is the janitor — all earlier hypotheses collapse

This illustrates how we build and revise models of meaning based on new evidence — a challenge for any NLP system.

#### Coherence vs. Cohesion

|Term|Definition|Example|
|---|---|---|
|**Coherence**|Text "makes sense" — there's logical structure|"Indoor climbing is good exercise. It gives a whole-body workout." ✅|
|**Incoherence**|Parts do not relate to each other|"Indoor climbing is good exercise. Rabbits are cute." ❌|
|**Cohesion**|Use of linguistic devices to tie text together|Repeating "police officers" → "Officers" in a passage|

#### Anaphora, Cataphora, and Coreference

- **Anaphora:** A pronoun referring BACK to a previously mentioned entity.
    
    - "The empress has not arrived but **she** should be here" → `she` refers back to `the empress`
    - `she` = **anaphor**; `the empress` = **antecedent**
- **Cataphora:** A pronoun that comes BEFORE its antecedent.
    
    - "Because **she** was going to the post office, Madhu was asked to post a parcel" → `she` anticipates `Madhu`
- **Coreference Chain:** Multiple expressions referring to the same real-world entity across a passage.

## 3. Natural Language Understanding (NLU) — Deep Dive

NLU manages **choices about interpretation** and handles **ill-formed input**. It differs from NLG in fundamental ways:

|NLU|NLG|
|---|---|
|Manages choices about _interpretation_|Manages choices about _realization_|
|Handles ill-formed (bad grammar) input|Must produce stylistically appropriate output|
|Input is text; output is meaning|Input is meaning; output is understandable text|

## 4. Natural Language Generation (NLG)

### Definition

NLG (McDonald, 1992): _"the process of deliberately constructing a natural language text in order to meet specified communicative goals."_

- **Input:** Non-linguistic representation of information
- **Output:** Text, hypertext, or speech

### Applications

- Automated document production (weather forecasts, letters)
- Dialogue systems
- Text summarization
- Machine translation
- Question answering

### The NLG Pipeline (Three-Stage Architecture)

```
[INPUT DATA]
     ↓
Text Planning ─────────── What to say?
     ↓                    (Content Determination + Discourse Planning)
Sentence Planning ──────── How to say it?
(Micro Planning)           (Aggregation + Lexicalization + REF Gen)
     ↓
Linguistic Realization ─── Surface form
(Surface Realization)      (Morphology + Syntax + Orthography)
     ↓
[OUTPUT TEXT]
```

### Stage 1: Content Determination

Decides **what to say** from communicative goals. Constructs a set of **messages** from the underlying data source.

Example messages for a train query:

- `DEPARTURETIME(CHENNAI_EXPRESS, 1000)` → "The Chennai Express leaves at 10am"
- `ID(NEXTTRAIN, CHENNAI_EXPRESS)` → "The next train is the Chennai Express"
- `COUNT(TRAIN, SRC(BANGALORE), DEST(CHENNAI), 10, PERDAY)` → "There are 10 trains daily from Bangalore to Chennai"

### Stage 2: Discourse Planning

A text is not a random collection of sentences — it has an **underlying structure**. Discourse planning arranges messages in a coherent structure (a **text plan**).

Example:

```
Sequence
├── COUNT(…) — "There are 10 trains daily..."
└── NextTrainInformation
    ├── Elaboration
    ├── IDENTITY(…) — "The next train is..."
    └── DEPARTURETIME(…) — "It leaves at..."
```

### Stage 3a: Sentence Planning — Aggregation

One message per sentence produces **disfluent** text. Aggregation combines messages into more natural, complex sentences.

- **Without aggregation:** "The next train is the CHENNAI Express. It leaves BANGALORE at 10am."
- **With aggregation:** "The next train, which leaves at 10am, is the CHENNAI Express."

### Stage 3b: Lexicalization

Determines which **specific words** to use for concepts and relations:

- Should departure time be expressed as _"leave"_ or _"depart"_?
- "A small amount of rain" vs. "Light rain" — same meaning, different register

### Stage 3c: Referring Expression Generation

Decides **how to refer to entities** so the reader knows what is being talked about. Choice between:

- Proper names (John, Dr. Smith)
- Definite descriptions (the tall man)
- Pronouns (he, she, it)

Major issue: **avoiding ambiguity** — "John hit Bill. He cried out." — who is He?

### Stage 4: Syntactic and Morphological Realization

- Morphology: walk + ed = walked
- Syntax: subject before the verb; determiner before noun

### Stage 5: Orthographic Realization

Case, punctuation, font size, layout decisions.

### Quality of NLG Output (Levels)

1. ✅ Clear meaning, good grammar and structure
2. ⚠️ Clear meaning but bad grammar
3. ⚠️ Meaning graspable but some ambiguity
4. ❌ Meaning unclear but inferable
5. ❌ Meaning absolutely unclear

## 5. POS Tagging

### Definition

**Part-of-Speech (POS) Tagging** is the process of assigning a grammatical category marker to each word in a corpus.

```
the   → DET
koala → N
put   → V
the   → DET
keys  → N
on    → P
the   → DET
table → N
```

### Why It Matters

POS tagging is used as a substep for:

- Partial (shallow) parsing
- Information extraction
- Question answering
- Input to statistical/regular parsers (significantly reduces running time)

### Popular Tagsets

|Tagset|Tags|
|---|---|
|Brown Corpus|87 tags|
|Penn Treebank|45 tags|
|Lancaster UCREL C5|61 tags|
|Lancaster C7|145 tags|

### The Core Problem: Ambiguity

One word can have multiple POS tags:

|Sentence|Tag|
|---|---|
|The **back** door|JJ (adjective)|
|On my **back**|NN (noun)|
|Win the voters **back**|RB (adverb)|
|Promised to **back** the bill|VB (verb)|

### Open vs. Closed Class Words

|Type|Description|Examples|
|---|---|---|
|**Closed class**|Fixed membership — no new words can be added|Prepositions (of, in), Auxiliaries (may, can), Pronouns (I, she)|
|**Open class**|New words can be created|Nouns, Verbs, Adjectives, Adverbs|

English has **4 open classes:** Nouns, Verbs, Adjectives, Adverbs.

### Methods of POS Tagging

#### Method 1: Rule-Based Tagging (e.g., ENGTWOL)

1. Start with a dictionary that lists every possible tag for each word
2. Apply rules to eliminate impossible tags given the context

Example: "Eliminate VBN if VBD is an option when preceded by `<start> PRP`"

#### Method 2: Statistical (Most Frequent Tag)

- For each word, count how many times each tag appeared in a tagged corpus
- Assign the most frequent tag for each word token

Example from Brown corpus: `P(Verb | race) = 96/98 = 0.98` → assign VB to "race"

#### Method 3: HMM (Hidden Markov Model) Tagger

**Goal:** Find the most probable tag sequence given the word sequence.

Uses two probabilities:

- **P(word | tag)** — lexical likelihood (word-tag matrix)
- **P(tag | previous n tags)** — tag sequence likelihood (tag-tag transition matrix)

The combined formula: $$\text{Best tags} = \arg\max P(\text{word seq} | \text{tag seq}) \times P(\text{tag} | \text{previous n tags})$$

**HMM Issues:**

1. Unknown words (no probabilities available)
2. Tag indeterminacy (token has multiple possible tags)
3. Tagging multi-part words (e.g., "New York")

#### Method 4: Neural / RNN Tagger

- Use tagged corpus (e.g., NLTK's `treebank.tagged_sents()` — ~3914 sentences, ~100,676 tagged words)
- Represent each word as a vector (using vocabulary encoding)
- Build a **bidirectional RNN** (works better than unidirectional)
- Output layer: softmax over all possible tags
- Loss: categorical cross-entropy; Optimizer: ADAM

### 📝 Q&A — Topic 5

**Q18: What is POS tagging and why is it a substep?**

> **A:** POS tagging assigns a grammatical category (noun, verb, etc.) to each word in a corpus. It is a substep because it feeds into higher NLP tasks like parsing, information extraction, and question answering.

**Q19: Explain the difference between open and closed class words.**

> **A:** Closed class words (prepositions, pronouns, auxiliaries) have a fixed set — new ones are rarely added. Open class words (nouns, verbs, adjectives, adverbs) can always grow as language evolves.

**Q20: How does the HMM tagger work?**

> **A:** It maximizes the product of P(word|tag) — the likelihood a tag produces the word — and P(tag|previous n tags) — the likelihood of a tag sequence. This uses a word-tag matrix and a tag-tag transition matrix, both estimated from a tagged corpus.

**Q21: Why is a bidirectional RNN better for POS tagging than a unidirectional one?**

> **A:** In POS tagging, context from both before and after a word helps disambiguate its tag. A bidirectional RNN processes the sentence both left-to-right and right-to-left, so each word's tag can be informed by full context.

**Q22: What is the most frequent tag baseline and when does it fail?**

> **A:** It assigns each word its most common POS tag from the training corpus. It fails for context-dependent words like "race" in "I race tomorrow" (verb) vs. "the race was close" (noun), since the most frequent tag alone is insufficient.

## 6. Text Segmentation & Information Extraction

### Text Segmentation

**Text segmentation** is the task of extracting relevant sub-units (words, phrases, sentences, paragraphs) from text.

Levels of segmentation:

- **Word segmentation** — splitting a character stream into words
- **Sentence boundary detection** — determining where sentences end
- **Tokenization** — splitting text into tokens (may differ from words)

**Challenges:**

- Ambiguity in punctuation: "Dr. Smith is here." — is the period after "Dr." a sentence boundary?
- Language-specific rules: Chinese and Arabic tokenization differs fundamentally from English

### Information Extraction (IE)

**Definition:** Finding factual information in free text and converting it into structured records.

A fact is a structured object: a record capturing a real-world entity with its attributes, actors, location, time, etc.

**Example:**

> "Three bombs have exploded in north-eastern Nigeria, killing 25 people..."

→ Extracted:

```
TYPE: Crisis; SUBTYPE: BOMBING
LOCATION: Maiduguri
DEAD_COUNT: 25; INJURED_COUNT: 12
PERPETRATOR: Islamic sect
WEAPONS: Bomb
TIME: Sunday afternoon
```

### IE vs. NLU vs. IR

|System|Goal|Scope|
|---|---|---|
|**IE**|Extract specific structured facts|Narrow — specific target setting|
|**NLU**|Full understanding of text|Broad — complete semantic analysis|
|**IR (Information Retrieval)**|Retrieve relevant documents for a query|Returns a ranked list of documents|

Key difference from IR: IE does not return ranked documents — it processes text into a structured form.

### Classic IE Tasks

#### 1. Named Entity Recognition (NER)

Identifying and classifying predefined named entities:

- Organizations: "World Health Organisation"
- Persons: "Muammar Kaddafi"
- Places: "the Baltic Sea"
- Temporal: "1 September 2011"
- Numerical/Currency: "20 Million Euros"

**Challenges — grey areas due to metonymy:**

- Person vs. Artifact
- Organisation vs. Location
- Company vs. Artifact

#### 2. Coreference Resolution

Identifying multiple mentions of the same entity:

- **Named:** "General Electric" and "GE" → same entity
- **Pronominal:** "John bought food. But **he** forgot drinks." → he = John
- **Nominal:** "Microsoft unveiled plans. **The company** also..." → The company = Microsoft
- **Implicit/Zero-anaphora:** "[He] flew over with a helicopter" (subject implied from context)

#### 3. Relation Extraction

Detecting and classifying relationships between named entities:

- `EmployeeOf(Steve Jobs, Apple)` — from "Steve Jobs works for Apple"
- `LocatedIn(Smith, New York)` — from "Mr. Smith gave a talk in New York"

Relations form the basis of **knowledge graphs**.

#### 4. Event Extraction

The hardest IE task — identifying events and their detailed information (who did what to whom, when, where, why, and how).

Example: Joint venture extraction — identify the partners, products, profits, and capitalization.

### Challenges in IE

1. Multi-linguality
2. Multi-source extraction
3. Social media (short, ungrammatical, unstructured text)
4. Open-domain IE (no predefined relations — extract everything)

**Q23: What is the difference between IE and IR?**

> **A:** Information Retrieval returns a ranked list of documents relevant to a query. Information Extraction processes text collections to extract structured facts — it doesn't return documents but converts unstructured text into structured data (like database records).

**Q24: Why is Event Extraction considered the hardest IE task?**

**Q26: What is relation extraction and what can be built from it?**

> **A:** Relation extraction detects and classifies relationships between entities in text (e.g., EmployeeOf, LocatedIn). The extracted relations, expressed as tuples, can be assembled into knowledge graphs.

## 7. Named Entity Recognition (NER)

### Definition

NER (also called entity chunking, extraction, or identification) is the task of:

1. **Detecting** a named entity
2. **Categorizing** it into a predefined class

An entity is any word or series of words that consistently refers to the same real-world thing.

**Example:**

> "[Apple]_ORG was founded by [Steve Jobs]_PERSON in [Cupertino]_LOCATION in [1976]_DATE."

### The Two-Step Process

**Step 1 — Detect:** Find tokens forming an entity. "The Great Lakes" = 3 tokens, 1 entity.

**Step 2 — Categorize:** Assign to a class (PERSON, ORG, LOCATION, DATE, CURRENCY, etc.)

### Applications

- **HR:** Summarize CVs, categorize employee queries
- **Customer Support:** Filter priority keywords from requests
- **Search Engines:** Improve result relevance using entity-aware retrieval
- **Healthcare:** Extract key info from lab reports
- **Academia:** Summarize research papers, highlight key terms

### NER Methods

1. **Rule-Based** — handcrafted patterns and gazetteers
2. **Machine Learning:**
    - Hidden Markov Model (HMM)
    - Naïve Bayes
    - Maximum Entropy Markov Model (MEMM)
    - **Conditional Random Fields (CRF)** — sequential classifier, considers word sequences
3. **Hybrid** — combines rules and ML

### NER in Code (NLTK & Stanford)

- NLTK: `ne_chunk()` — needs POS annotations; uses a maximum entropy chunker trained on ACE corpus; does NOT handle dates/times well
- **Stanford NER:** Java-based, uses CRF Classifier → better at dates/times; CRF is a sequential classifier, so it takes word sequences into account

---

### 📝 Q&A — Topic 7

**Q27: What makes NER challenging — what are the grey areas?**

> **A:** Many grey areas are caused by metonymy (e.g., "London" can be a Location or an Organization as in the city government). Categories like Person vs. Artifact, Organisation vs. Location overlap in real usage.

**Q28: Why is CRF preferred over simple classifiers for NER?**

> **A:** CRF is a sequential classifier — it considers the entire word sequence rather than classifying each token independently. This allows it to capture context from surrounding tokens, which is crucial for ambiguous entities.

**Q29: What is the difference between entity detection and entity categorization in NER?**

> **A:** Detection identifies which tokens form an entity boundary (e.g., "Great Lakes" as a multi-token span). Categorization assigns the detected span to a predefined class (LOCATION, PERSON, etc.).

**Q30: Give two applications of NER from very different industries.**

> **A:** Healthcare — extracting patient names, diagnoses, dates, and medication from clinical notes. HR — scanning CVs to extract candidate names, skills, institutions, and work experience automatically.

## 8. Coreference Resolution

### Definition

Coreference resolution is a **clustering task** that identifies all noun phrases (mentions) in a document that refer to the same real-world entity.

**Example:**

> "[I] gave [my] book to [Anna]. [She] thanked [me]."

- {I, my, me} → Cluster 1 (the speaker)
- {Anna, She} → Cluster 2 (Anna)

### Why It Matters

Coreference is a prerequisite for:

- Question answering
- Document summarization
- Machine translation

### The Two-Stage Process

#### Stage 1: Mention Detection

Find all **candidate spans** that could refer to entities. Three types:

1. **Pronouns** — I, she, he, they, it...
2. **NER entities** — detected and classified entities
3. **Noun Phrases** — groups of words headed by a noun

#### Stage 2: Mention Clustering

Group mentions that refer to the same entity. Merge into coreference clusters for each real-world entity in the text.

### Key Terms

|Term|Definition|
|---|---|
|**Antecedent**|The expression that gives meaning to a pronoun — in "Gal was happy. She smiled," `Gal` is the antecedent of `she`|
|**Anaphor**|The pronoun/expression whose meaning depends on the antecedent|
|**Anaphora**|Reference back to a prior expression|
|**Cataphora**|Pronoun appears before its antecedent|

### Candidate Referent Disambiguation

"John saw Mary in the park. As every morning, **she** was walking her dog."

Candidates for "she":

- John → ❌ gender mismatch (he)
- **Mary → ✅ gender match**
- The park → ❌ location, not a person
- Every morning → ❌ time expression
- Her dog → ❌ animal, not human

### Coreference Resolution System Components

1. **A discourse model** — dynamically represents entities and relationships encountered so far
2. **Two methods:**
    - Constructing/updating the discourse model as the text is processed
    - Mapping referring expressions to their referents in the discourse model

---

### 📝 Q&A — Topic 8

**Q31: What is the difference between coreference resolution and anaphora resolution?**

> **A:** Anaphora resolution resolves a specific pronoun to its antecedent. Coreference resolution is broader — it clusters ALL referring expressions that refer to the same entity, including named mentions, pronouns, and noun phrases.

**Q32: Why is coreference resolution a clustering task?**

> **A:** Because the goal is not just to link a single pronoun to a noun, but to build clusters of ALL mentions of the same entity across the document. Each cluster corresponds to one real-world entity.

**Q33: What are the two sub-tasks of coreference resolution?**

> **A:** (1) Mention Detection — find all candidate spans that could refer to entities. (2) Mention Clustering — determine which mentions refer to the same entity and group them together.

**Q34: In "John saw Mary. She smiled," what is the anaphor, what is the antecedent, and which referent resolution step resolves this?**

> **A:** The anaphor is "She." The antecedent is "Mary." This is resolved in the mention clustering step after gender information from NER confirms Mary (female) matches "She."

## 9. Dependency Parsing & Relation Extraction

### Dependency Parsing

**Definition:** The process of analyzing grammatical structure in a sentence by finding **head-dependent relationships** between words.

Every word pair in a dependency tree has:

1. A **head** (the governor)
2. A **dependent** (the modifier)
3. A **label** describing the relationship type

**Example:** "The black car"

- `car` ← **head**
- `black` ← **dependent**
- `amod` ← label (adjectival modifier)

Dependency labels come from [universaldependencies.org](https://universaldependencies.org/u/dep/).

### Applications of Dependency Parsing

- **Question Answering:** Understanding entity relationships makes it easier to answer "Who owned what?"
- **Machine Translation:** Maintaining grammatical agreement across languages

### Relation Extraction

**Definition:** Identifying **relationships** between entities in text and expressing them as structured tuples.

**Example:**

> "Barack Obama was born in Honolulu."

- Entity 1: Barack Obama
- Relation: Born_in
- Entity 2: Honolulu

### Three Approaches to Relation Extraction

|Approach|How It Works|
|---|---|
|**Rule-Based**|Manually defined syntactic/semantic patterns. Uses grammatical structure to identify word dependencies.|
|**Pattern/ML-Based**|Treated as classification — annotated data, entity pair identification. Uses Logistic Regression, Random Forests, RNNs, Transformers|
|**Unsupervised (OpenIE)**|No labeled data or predefined schemas. Extracts relations as strings starting with verbs. Open-domain.|

---

### 📝 Q&A — Topic 9

**Q35: What is dependency parsing and what is a "label" in this context?**

> **A:** Dependency parsing finds head-dependent relationships between words in a sentence. A label describes the nature of the dependency (e.g., `amod` = adjectival modifier, `nsubj` = nominal subject).

**Q36: How does OpenIE differ from supervised relation extraction?**

> **A:** OpenIE requires no labeled data or predefined relation schema — it extracts any relation it finds, typically represented as verb strings. Supervised RE requires labeled entity pairs and works on a closed set of predefined relation types.

**Q37: What is the output of relation extraction, and what larger structure can be built from it?**

> **A:** The output is a set of typed tuples: (Entity1, Relation, Entity2). These tuples can be assembled into a **knowledge graph**, a structured representation of world knowledge.

## 10. Language Models

### What is a Language Model?

A **language model (LM)** assigns a probability to a sequence of words:

$$P(w_1, w_2, w_3, \ldots, w_n)$$

It answers: _How likely is this sequence of words?_ You use language models every day — autocomplete, spell check, voice assistants all rely on them.

### Why Language Models Matter

Language models are foundational to:

- Machine translation
- Speech recognition
- Spelling correction
- Text generation
- Named entity recognition
- Question answering

### The Noisy Channel Framework

The Noisy Channel is a powerful unifying framework for NLP tasks. The intuition:

```
True Signal (Y) --[Noisy Channel]--> Observed Signal (X)
```

We observe X (the noisy, corrupted signal) and want to recover Y (the true signal). By Bayes' theorem:

$$\hat{Y} = \arg\max_Y P(Y|X) = \arg\max_Y P(X|Y) \cdot P(Y)$$

This requires:

- **P(Y)** — a language model (the prior over correct sequences)
- **P(X|Y)** — a noise model (task-specific)

|Application|Y (True)|X (Observed)|Noise Model|
|---|---|---|---|
|Speech Recognition|True words|Acoustic signals|Acoustic model|
|Machine Translation|Source language|Target language|Translation model|
|Spelling Correction|Correct words|Typed (misspelled) words|Keyboard/typo model|

### Two Types of Language Models

|Type|Description|Examples|
|---|---|---|
|**Count-Based (N-gram)**|Statistical; use frequencies of word sequences|Unigram, Bigram, Trigram|
|**Continuous-Space (Neural)**|Use word embeddings; address sparsity problems|Feed-forward LM, RNN LM, Transformer LM|

**Issues with Count-Based Models:**

1. **Curse of Dimensionality** — the number of possible n-grams is astronomically large
2. **Context window limited** — only considers last n-1 words
3. **Exact pattern matching** — "cat" and "dog" are semantically similar pets but treated as completely unrelated

---

### 📝 Q&A — Topic 10

**Q38: What is a language model formally?**

> **A:** A language model assigns a probability P(W) to a sequence of words W = w₁, w₂, ..., wₙ. Higher probability → the model considers the sequence more likely to be natural language.

**Q39: Explain the Noisy Channel framework and give one example.**

> **A:** The framework models an observation X as a true signal Y passed through a noisy channel. We recover Y = argmax P(Y|X) = argmax P(X|Y)·P(Y). In spell correction: Y = the correct word the user intended, X = the misspelled word they typed. P(Y) is the language model; P(X|Y) is the typo model.

**Q40: What are the three core issues with N-gram language models?**

> **A:** (1) Curse of Dimensionality — too many possible n-gram combinations. (2) Limited context — only the last n-1 words are considered. (3) Exact pattern matching — semantically similar words (cat, dog) are treated as entirely different, unlike neural embeddings.

## 11. N-gram Language Models & Smoothing

### How N-gram Models Work

An **N-gram** is a sequence of N consecutive words. We estimate sentence probability using the **chain rule + Markov assumption**:

$$P(w_n | w_1 \ldots w_{n-1}) \approx P(w_n | w_{n-N+1} \ldots w_{n-1})$$

Only the last **N-1 words** are treated as context.

**Estimating from a corpus:**

$$P(w_n | w_{n-1}) = \frac{C(w_{n-1}, w_n)}{C(w_{n-1})}$$

(count of bigram / count of preceding word)

### The Sparsity Problem

N-gram models require a large corpus, but no matter how large:

- Shakespeare had 884,647 tokens and 29,066 unique words → only 300,000 bigram types out of **844 million possible** → 99.96% of bigrams never seen
- **Zero probability problem:** If a bigram never appeared in training, its probability = 0
- **This breaks perplexity calculation and any downstream task** (same problem as in Naïve Bayes)
- We work **in log space** to avoid underflow

### Words follow a **Zipfian Distribution**

No matter how large the training corpus, there will always be many rare or unseen events. This is the fundamental motivation for smoothing.

---

### Smoothing Techniques

Smoothing **redistributes probability mass** from seen n-grams to unseen ones, ensuring no zero probabilities.

#### 1. Laplace (Add-1) Smoothing

Add 1 to every count:

$$P_{Laplace}(w_n|w_{n-1}) = \frac{C(w_{n-1}, w_n) + 1}{C(w_{n-1}) + V}$$

Where V = vocabulary size.

**Laplace is technically a Bayesian MAP estimator with a uniform prior, not pure MLE.**

**Problem:** Too blunt. For large, sparse vocabularies, it gives far too much probability mass to unseen events. Not used for N-grams in practice, but used for other sparse NLP models.

#### 2. Add-K Smoothing (Lidstone's Law / Add-ε)

$$P_{AddK}(w_n|w_{n-1}) = \frac{C(w_{n-1}, w_n) + k}{C(w_{n-1}) + kV}$$

- Works better than Add-1 but still problematic
- K (or m = KV) is chosen on a **held-out set** (train → held-out → test)

Setting m/V = k relates to **unigram prior smoothing**.

#### 3. Good-Turing Smoothing

**Robin Hood intuition:** Take probability mass from n-grams that occur **r+1 times** and give it to those that occur **r times**.

**Frequency of frequency (N_c):** The number of n-grams that occurred exactly c times.

**Effective count formula:**

$$c^* = \frac{(c+1) \cdot N_{c+1}}{N_c}$$

For unseen n-grams (c=0): $$P(\text{unseen}) = \frac{N_1}{N \cdot N_0}$$

**Issues with Good-Turing:**

- For large k: N_k and N_{k+1} are both small → estimates are too jumpy
- Church & Gale (1991) found that c* ≈ c - 0.75 for most counts → **Absolute Discounting**

#### 4. Kneser-Ney Smoothing

Improves Good-Turing by changing the **donor** of probability mass.

**Key Insight:** "I can't see without my reading ___"

- "Francisco" has higher unigram frequency than "glasses"
- BUT "Francisco" almost always follows "San," while "glasses" appears after MANY different words
- So "glasses" is a better novel continuation

**Novel continuation probability** = number of bigram _types_ a word completes.

Instead of using simple unigram probability as a backoff, we use how likely a word is to appear as a **novel continuation**.

#### 5. Mixed Strategies

**Problem:** As n increases, model power increases but sparsity also increases.

**Solution:** Use both low-n and high-n models together.

**Backoff:**

- Use trigram if you have reliable data
- Else back off to bigram
- Else back off to unigram
- "Try to fill any gap in n-grams at level n by looking back to level n-1"

**Interpolation:**

- Always mix unigram + bigram + trigram estimates with learned weights λ:

$$P(w_n | w_{n-2}, w_{n-1}) = \lambda_1 P(w_n) + \lambda_2 P(w_n|w_{n-1}) + \lambda_3 P(w_n|w_{n-2}, w_{n-1})$$

Where λ₁ + λ₂ + λ₃ = 1. Lambdas are found using a **held-out corpus**.

**Intuition:** Trigram estimates capture context but need lots of data to converge. Unigram estimates have no context but converge quickly. Bigrams are in between.

### 📝 Q&A — Topic 11

**Q41: Why do N-gram models have a zero-probability problem and why does it matter?**

> **A:** If an n-gram never appeared in training, its count is 0, so its probability is 0. In perplexity computation (which uses log probabilities), log(0) is undefined. In the noisy channel, any sequence containing a zero-probability n-gram gets a total probability of zero, breaking the model.

**Q42: Is Laplace smoothing an MLE estimate? Explain.**

> **A:** No. It is a Bayesian MAP estimate with a uniform prior. It modifies the standard MLE formula by adding a uniform prior count, which turns it into a MAP estimate. "MLE" is loosely used for it because it approximates MLE while solving the zero-count problem.

**Q43: Explain the Kneser-Ney intuition with the "San Francisco" example.**

> **A:** "Francisco" has high unigram frequency but almost always follows "San." "Glasses" has lower frequency but appears after many different words — it has higher "novel continuation" probability. Kneser-Ney uses this diversity of contexts as the probability donor instead of raw unigram counts.

**Q44: What is the difference between Backoff and Interpolation?**

> **A:** Backoff uses a lower-order model ONLY when the higher-order model has zero/insufficient data. Interpolation ALWAYS mixes all order models (unigram + bigram + trigram) with learned weights, even when the higher-order model has data.

**Q45: What is "Frequency of Frequency" and why is it needed for Good-Turing?**

> **A:** N_c = the number of n-grams that appear exactly c times. It is needed because Good-Turing reallocates probability mass from (c+1)-count n-grams to c-count n-grams. The formula c* = (c+1)·N_{c+1}/N_c requires knowing how many n-grams fall in each frequency bin.

**Q46: What is absolute discounting?**

> **A:** Based on Church & Gale's empirical finding that c* ≈ c − 0.75 for most counts. This means instead of using the full Good-Turing formula, a fixed discount of 0.75 is subtracted from each count and the saved probability mass is redistributed.

## 12. Perplexity

### What is Perplexity?

Perplexity is the standard **intrinsic evaluation metric** for language models. It measures how well a language model _predicts_ a test set.

> "The **weighted average number of choices** the model thinks it has at each decision point."

A perplexity of 10 means the model, on average, is choosing between 10 equally likely words at each step.

**Key insight:** Higher probability assigned → Lower perplexity → Better model.

$$\text{Higher } P(W) \Rightarrow \text{Lower Perplexity} \Rightarrow \text{Better LM}$$

### The Problem Perplexity Solves

You cannot directly compare sentence probabilities because longer sentences have lower probabilities (more multiplications of sub-1 numbers). Perplexity **normalizes by sentence length** using the geometric mean.

### Mathematical Formula

$$PP(W) = P(w_1, w_2, \ldots, w_N)^{-1/N}$$

Applying the chain rule:

$$PP(W) = \sqrt[N]{\prod_{i=1}^{N} \frac{1}{P(w_i | w_1 \ldots w_{i-1})}}$$

For a bigram model:

$$PP(W) = \sqrt[N]{\prod_{i=1}^{N} \frac{1}{P(w_i | w_{i-1})}}$$

### Perplexity as a Branching Factor

For a random digit model assigning P = 1/10 to each digit (uniform unigram):

$$PP = \left(\frac{1}{10}\right)^{-1} = 10$$

The **dumbest possible language model** with vocabulary V and uniform distribution has perplexity = V.

### Extrinsic vs. Intrinsic Evaluation

|Type|Definition|Pros|Cons|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Extrinsic**|Evaluate in a downstream task (e.g., spell corrector accuracy)|Gold standard|Very time-consuming (days/weeks)|
|**Intrinsic**|Perplexity on a test set|Fast, comparable across models|Only useful if test data resembles training data|

### Perplexity vs. Cross-Entropy

They are directly related:

$$\text{Perplexity}(p, q) = 2^{H(p,q)}$$

Where H(p,q) is cross-entropy. Cross-entropy gives the answer in **bits**; perplexity transforms it to a more interpretable **branching factor**.

---

### 📝 Q&A — Topic 12

**Q47: Why can't we simply compare sentence probabilities to evaluate language models?**

> **A:** Longer sentences always have lower probabilities because the total probability is the product of many sub-1 numbers. A 20-word sentence will almost always have lower probability than a 5-word sentence, even if the model predicts the longer sentence perfectly.

**Q48: What does a perplexity of 100 mean in plain language?**

> **A:** On average, the model is uncertain between 100 equally likely words at each prediction step. A perplexity of 100 is much worse than a perplexity of 10 — lower is better.

**Q49: What is the perplexity of the "dumbest" language model?**

> **A:** If every word in a vocabulary of size V has equal probability 1/V, the perplexity equals V. This is the worst-case perplexity for a given vocabulary.

**Q50: What is the mathematical relationship between perplexity and cross-entropy?**

> **A:** Perplexity(p,q) = 2^H(p,q), where H(p,q) is the cross-entropy between the true distribution p and the model distribution q. Both measure the same thing — cross-entropy in bits, perplexity as a branching factor.

## 13. Word Embeddings — Sparse (TF-IDF, PMI)

### Distributional Hypothesis

The foundation of all word vector approaches:

> **"Words that occur in the same context tend to have similar meanings."** — Zellig Harris **"Meaning of a word is its use in language."** — Wittgenstein, 1953

Semantically similar words → similar distributional patterns → similar vector representations.

**Important caveat:** Distributional semantics captures **differential meaning** (the difference between "train" and "car") rather than **referential meaning** (what "train" actually IS).

---

### TF-IDF (Term Frequency — Inverse Document Frequency)

Used to create a sparse vector representation of documents. Captures word importance in context.

#### Term Frequency (TF)

Raw frequency is not ideal — a word appearing 10 times is not 10x more relevant. Use **log-frequency weighting**:

$$w_{t,d} = \begin{cases} 1 + \log_{10}(tf_{t,d}) & \text{if } tf_{t,d} > 0 \ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

Scale: 0 → 0, 1 → 1, 2 → 1.3, 10 → 2, 1000 → 4

#### Inverse Document Frequency (IDF)

Rare terms are more informative than frequent terms. IDF penalizes common words:

$$idf_t = \log_{10}\left(\frac{N}{df_t}\right)$$

Where N = total documents, df_t = number of documents containing t.

|Term|df_t|idf_t|
|---|---|---|
|calpurnia|1|6|
|animal|100|4|
|sunday|1,000|3|
|the|1,000,000|0|

"the" has idf = 0 — it provides no discriminative power.

#### TF-IDF Weight

$$w_{t,d} = \log(1 + tf_{t,d}) \times \log_{10}(N/df_t)$$

Increases with:

- Number of occurrences in the document
- Rarity of the term across the collection

**Note:** IDF has NO effect on single-term queries. It matters only for multi-term queries.

#### Document-Query Scoring

$$\text{Score}(q, d) = \sum_{t \in q \cap d} tf\text{-}idf_{t,d}$$

---

### PMI — Pointwise Mutual Information

PMI measures the **statistical association** between two words x and y:

$$\text{PMI}(x, y) = \log_2 \frac{P(x, y)}{P(x) \cdot P(y)}$$

**Interpretation:**

- PMI > 0 → words co-occur more often than chance → strong association
- PMI = 0 → statistically independent
- PMI < 0 → words avoid each other → negative association

**PPMI (Positive PMI):** Replace negative PMI values with 0.

#### PMI vs. TF-IDF: When to Use Which

|Metric|Best For|
|---|---|
|**TF-IDF**|Finding importance of words within documents; classification where word frequency relative to corpus matters|
|**PMI**|Capturing semantic associations; document classification where distributional co-occurrence patterns matter more than raw frequency|

---

### 📝 Q&A — Topic 13

**Q51: State the Distributional Hypothesis and explain what it tells us.**

> **A:** "Words that occur in the same context tend to have similar meanings." It tells us that we can represent a word's meaning by the distribution of words that appear around it — no need for a dictionary definition. Semantically similar words (cat, dog) should have similar context distributions.

**Q52: Why is log-frequency weighting used instead of raw TF?**

> **A:** A document with 10 occurrences of a word is not 10 times more relevant than one with 1 occurrence. Relevance grows sublinearly. Log dampens this growth, making the weighting more realistic.

**Q53: Explain why "the" has an IDF value of 0.**

> **A:** "the" appears in nearly every document (df_t ≈ N), so log(N/df_t) ≈ log(1) = 0. A zero IDF means the word provides no discriminative information between documents.

**Q54: If PMI between two words is very high, what does that imply?**

> **A:** The words co-occur much more often than would be expected if they were statistically independent. This indicates a strong semantic or syntactic association — for example, "ice" and "cold" would have high PMI.

**Q55: What is PPMI and why is it useful?**

> **A:** Positive PMI (PPMI) replaces all negative PMI values with 0. Negative PMI values are unreliable (particularly for rare events) and often misleading, so PPMI focuses only on positive associations.

## 14. Word Embeddings — Dense (Word2Vec, GloVe, FastText)

### Motivation: From Sparse to Dense

**Sparse embeddings (TF-IDF, PMI):**

- Size = |Vocabulary| — often 50,000+ dimensions
- Mostly zeros
- No semantic relationship between vectors (cat and dog are orthogonal)

**Dense embeddings:**

- Size = 50–300 dimensions (a hyperparameter)
- All dimensions have values
- Semantically similar words have similar vectors (cat and dog are close in embedding space)
- Enable arithmetic: king − man + woman ≈ queen

---

### Word2Vec

Word2Vec learns dense word representations by training a **shallow neural network** to predict word contexts. Two architectures:

#### CBOW (Continuous Bag-of-Words)

> **"Given the context words, predict the target word."**

- Input: surrounding context words (one-hot encoded, each 1×V)
- Hidden layer: average of context word vectors (1×N)
- Output: predicted target word (softmax over V)
- Training objective: maximize P(target | context words)

$$J(\theta) = \frac{1}{T} \sum_t \log P(w_t | w_{t-n}, \ldots, w_{t-1}, w_{t+1}, \ldots, w_{t+n})$$

#### Skip-gram

> **"Given the target word, predict the context words."**

- Input: single target word (1×V)
- Hidden layer: target word embedding (1×N)
- Output: C probability distributions over vocabulary (one per context position)
- Training objective: maximize probability of context words given the target

$$J(\theta) = \frac{1}{T} \sum_t \sum_j \log P(w_{t+j} | w_t)$$

#### CBOW vs. Skip-gram

|Feature|CBOW|Skip-gram|
|---|---|---|
|Context usage|Averages context vectors|Uses individual context words|
|Speed|Faster|Slower|
|Best for|Frequent words|Rare words, semantic relationships|
|Context weighting|Lower (averaged/smoothed)|Higher (each context word counted)|

#### What is the Embedding?

After training, the **input-hidden weight matrix W1 (V×N)** IS the embedding matrix. Each row corresponds to one word's dense vector representation.

#### Optimizations

**Hierarchical Softmax:** Instead of computing softmax over all V outputs, arrange vocabulary as leaves of a binary tree. Computing probability requires traversing from leaf to root → O(log₂V) instead of O(V).

**Negative Sampling:** Instead of updating all V output vectors, only update the positive example and a small sample of "incorrect" contexts (negative examples). Makes training much faster.

#### Limitations of Word2Vec

1. **Context collapse:** Cat and dog are similar (both pets) but cat and tiger should also be similar (both felines) — Word2Vec only captures common context
2. **Antonyms are similar:** "good" and "bad" appear in similar contexts → similar vectors
3. **Corpus bias:** Reflects biases in the training corpus
4. **Context-independent:** The same vector for "bank" regardless of whether it's a river bank or a financial bank
5. **Local context only:** Skip-gram only looks at a fixed window; ignores global corpus statistics

#### Doc2Vec

Extension of Word2Vec to learn **document-level embeddings** by adding a document ID vector as additional context. Enables finding most similar documents/paragraphs.

---

### GloVe (Global Vectors for Word Representation)

**Motivation:** Word2Vec looks at one window at a time (local context). GloVe captures **global co-occurrence statistics** across the entire corpus.

#### Two Types of Co-occurrence Counts

- **Window-based counts:** Captures syntactic and semantic information
- **Document-based counts:** Captures topic information → leads to LSA (Latent Semantic Analysis)

#### The Core Insight — Ratios of Probabilities

Consider words "ice" and "steam" and their co-occurrence probabilities with other words:

- P("solid"|"ice") >> P("solid"|"steam") → ratio >> 1 (ice is more solid)
- P("gas"|"steam") >> P("gas"|"ice") → ratio << 1 (steam is more gas-like)
- P("water"|"ice") ≈ P("water"|"steam") → ratio ≈ 1 (both are water-related)

Only the **ratios of probabilities** cleanly encode the relevant semantic distinctions.

#### Training Objective

GloVe trains word vectors such that:

$$v_w \cdot v_c \approx \log P(w, c)$$

The dot product of two word vectors approximates the log co-occurrence probability.

$$\log \frac{P(w, c_1)}{P(w, c_2)} = v_w \cdot (v_{c_1} - v_{c_2})$$

Vector **differences** in the embedding space encode co-occurrence **ratios**.

GloVe constructs the full co-occurrence matrix once across the corpus, then factorizes it (dimensionality reduction), training faster than Word2Vec.

---

### FastText

**Motivation:** Word2Vec and GloVe treat each word as an atomic unit. "running," "runs," "ran" are completely different → poor handling of morphology and OOV words.

#### Key Innovation: Subword Information

FastText represents each word as a **bag of character n-grams** plus the whole word:

"apple" → `<ap`, `app`, `ppl`, `ple`, `le>`, `apple`

The word's vector = sum of its n-gram vectors.

#### Advantages

1. **Captures morphology:** Shares information across "eat," "eats," "eaten," "eating" (common n-grams)
2. **Handles OOV words:** Even a word never seen can be represented from its character n-grams
3. **Parameter sharing:** Related word forms share n-gram vectors → robust with small corpora
4. **Language-independent:** No language-specific tools (stemmer, lemmatizer) needed
5. **Better syntactic performance:** Subword structure captures syntactic patterns

#### Architecture

Same as Word2Vec (CBOW or Skip-gram) but the input is n-gram vectors of the target word (not just the word itself).

---

### Choosing the Right Embedding Model

|Model|Use When|
|---|---|
|**Word2Vec**|Semantic relationships are crucial; large dataset available|
|**GloVe**|Diverse dataset; global context is important|
|**FastText**|Morphologically rich languages; OOV words are a concern; smaller corpora|

### 📝 Q&A — Topic 14

**Q56: What does the King − Man + Woman = Queen analogy demonstrate about word embeddings?**

> **A:** It demonstrates that dense word embeddings capture semantic and relational structure. The vector difference between "King" and "Man" encodes the concept of "royalty," and adding it to "Woman" yields a vector closest to "Queen." Meaning is encoded in directions in the vector space.

**Q57: In CBOW, what is the embedding actually stored in?**

> **A:** The embedding is stored in the input-hidden weight matrix W1 (size V×N). Each row of W1 corresponds to the N-dimensional embedding of one vocabulary word. After training, this matrix is the word embedding lookup table.

**Q58: Why does Skip-gram do better for rare words than CBOW?**

> **A:** CBOW averages the context vectors, which smooths out information — rare words' signals get diluted. Skip-gram trains the model to predict each context word individually from the target, so every occurrence of a rare word contributes distinct gradient signals.

**Q59: What is negative sampling and why is it needed?**

> **A:** Computing the softmax denominator over all V vocabulary words is expensive. Negative sampling approximates this by only updating the positive example (a real context word) plus a small random sample of "negative" (non-context) words. This makes training computationally feasible.

**Q60: How does GloVe differ from Word2Vec in terms of what statistics it uses?**

> **A:** Word2Vec uses local context windows and sees each window as a separate training example (local statistics). GloVe first computes a global co-occurrence matrix across the entire corpus, then factorizes it — capturing global distributional statistics in a single matrix.

**Q61: What specific problem does FastText solve that Word2Vec cannot handle?**

> **A:** Word2Vec cannot represent out-of-vocabulary (OOV) words and ignores internal word structure. FastText represents each word as a bag of character n-grams, so even unseen words can be given meaningful vectors by summing their known n-gram vectors.

**Q62: What is Doc2Vec and how does it extend Word2Vec?**

> **A:** Doc2Vec adds a document ID vector as an additional input alongside word context vectors. While word vectors capture word meanings, the document vector captures the unique concept/theme of each document. After training, similar documents will have similar document vectors.

## 15. CNNs for NLP

### Recap: How CNNs Work

A **convolution** is a sliding window function applied across an input. For images, it slides a small filter across a 2D pixel matrix. Two key properties:

1. **Location Invariance:** The filter detects the same feature regardless of where it appears (useful for finding an elephant anywhere in an image)
2. **Local Compositionality:** Build edges from pixels, shapes from edges, objects from shapes

### CNNs Applied to Text

For NLP, the input is **1-dimensional** (a sequence of word embedding vectors):

```
Input text: 7 words, each with a 5-dimensional embedding → 7×5 matrix

Filter:
- One dimension = embedding width (5) — always fixed
- Other dimension = window of words (K) — varies (e.g., K=2, K=3)

Convolution produces a feature map for each filter.
```

#### Architecture (Zhang et al., 2014)

1. **Embedding Layer:** Convert words to dense vectors (Word2Vec, GloVe)
2. **Convolution Layer:** Multiple filters of different sizes (bigram, trigram, 4-gram)
3. **1-Max Pooling:** Extract the largest value from each feature map → gives a fixed-length vector
4. **Concat:** Concatenate all pooled values
5. **Fully Connected + Softmax:** Classification

**Why 1-Max Pooling?** Feature maps vary in size depending on sentence length and filter size. Extracting the maximum makes the representation fixed-length and length-independent, focusing on the **most salient** feature regardless of position.

### Why CNNs are Efficient for N-grams

With a large vocabulary, computing 3-grams or higher directly is expensive. Convolutional filters **automatically learn** useful n-gram-like patterns without explicitly representing the entire vocabulary. Filters of size 5+ are common and practical.

### Filter Visualization

A filter of size 2 might activate for bigrams like "not good" or "very happy." A filter of size 5 might capture "not as bad as I expected." Filters act as **learned feature detectors**.

### Character-Level CNNs

Instead of words, model text at the **character level**:

- Input: one-hot encoded characters in an alphabet
- Advantage: No need for syntactic/semantic knowledge; handles misspellings, emoticons, OOV automatically

### Limitations of CNNs for NLP

1. **Weak long-range dependencies** — filters have fixed size; cannot model very distant word relationships
2. **Loss of sequential information** — pooling discards word order (problematic for sequence tagging)
3. **Fixed context window** — cannot flexibly capture variable contexts
4. **Limited hierarchical understanding** — compared to RNNs/Transformers for syntax/semantics
5. **Not ideal for variable-length output** — cannot directly generate sequences (for translation, summarization)

### CNNs as Feature Extractors

CNNs don't stand alone — they are **feature extraction modules** integrated into larger networks:

- **CNN + RNN (LRCN):** CNN extracts local spatial features; LSTM handles temporal structure
- **CNN + Attention:** CNN extracts local features; self-attention refines global context

### Best NLP Use Cases for CNNs

- ✅ Sentence/document classification (sentiment, spam, topic)
- ✅ Text matching
- ❌ Sequence tagging (POS, NER) — pooling loses word order
- ❌ Machine translation — needs variable-length output

### 📝 Q&A — Topic 15

**Q63: Why does location invariance apply to images but NOT naturally to NLP?**

> **A:** In images, a cat is a cat wherever it appears. In language, position matters — "The cat chased the dog" and "The dog chased the cat" have opposite meanings despite using the same words. Syntactic and semantic roles are position-dependent.

**Q64: What is the purpose of 1-max pooling in CNN-based text classification?**

> **A:** Different sentences have different lengths, producing feature maps of different sizes. 1-max pooling extracts the single maximum value from each feature map, creating a fixed-length representation regardless of input length. It also focuses the representation on the most salient feature detected by each filter.

**Q65: How does a CNN capture n-gram-like features in text?**

> **A:** A filter of window size K slides over K consecutive word vectors, performing an element-wise multiply-and-sum operation. This detects patterns across K words — analogous to K-grams. Multiple filters of different sizes capture patterns at different granularities (bigrams, trigrams, etc.).

**Q66: What are the two main advantages of character-level CNNs over word-level models?**

> **A:** (1) No need for vocabulary — any character sequence is representable. (2) Handles OOV words, misspellings, and informal language naturally since it operates on raw characters, not dictionary entries.

**Q67: What is the CNN-LSTM (LRCN) architecture and when is it useful?**

> **A:** CNN extracts local features (spatial or n-gram patterns), which are then fed into an LSTM for temporal/sequential modeling. Useful for tasks like gesture recognition, image/video captioning, or any task with both local structure and temporal dependencies.

## 16. Sequence Models & RNNs

### The Sequence Learning Problem

Many NLP tasks involve **variable-length inputs** where **successive inputs are NOT independent**:

|Task|Input sequence|Output|
|---|---|---|
|Auto-completion|Characters so far|Next character|
|POS Tagging|Words in sentence|Tag for each word|
|Sentiment Analysis|Entire sentence|+/- label|
|Machine Translation|Sentence in language A|Sentence in language B|

### Four Types of Sequence Problems

|Type|Pattern|Example|
|---|---|---|
|**Sequence Prediction**|Many → Many (same length)|POS tagging|
|**Sequence Generation**|One → Many|Image captioning|
|**Sequence Recognition**|Many → One|Sentiment analysis|
|**Sequential Decision Making**|Many → Many (different length)|Machine translation|

### Limitations of HMMs for Sequences

HMMs can only store **log(N) bits** of information about what they generated (where N = number of hidden states). The first half of a sentence needs to convey ~100 bits about syntax, semantics, intonation, etc. to the second half. 2^100 is astronomically large — HMMs fundamentally cannot handle this.

---

### Designing an RNN

An RNN must:

1. Account for dependencies between inputs
2. Handle variable-length inputs
3. Use the **same function at each time step** (parameter sharing)

#### The Key Equations

$$H_i = \tanh(X_i U + H_{i-1} W + b)$$

$$Y_i = \text{softmax}(V H_i + c)$$

Where:

- **X_i** = current input (word vector)
- **H_{i-1}** = previous hidden state (memory)
- **U** = input-to-hidden weights
- **W** = hidden-to-hidden weights (recurrent connection)
- **V** = hidden-to-output weights

**Parameter sharing (U, W, V are the same for every time step)** is what makes RNNs:

- Length-independent (can handle any sequence length)
- Dependent (H_i encodes all previous inputs)

#### RNN Architectures

|Architecture|Description|Example|
|---|---|---|
|**1-to-1**|Single input → single output at each step|Character prediction|
|**1-to-Many**|Single input → sequence output|Image captioning|
|**Many-to-1**|Sequence input → single output|Sentiment analysis|
|**Many-to-Many**|Sequence input → sequence output (different lengths)|Machine translation (Encoder-Decoder)|

#### Dimensions of RNN Components

If input has dimension D_x, hidden layer has dimension D_h, output has D_y:

- U: D_x × D_h
- W: D_h × D_h
- V: D_h × D_y

---

### 📝 Q&A — Topic 16

**Q68: What three requirements did we identify for a model to solve sequence learning problems?**

> **A:** (1) Account for dependencies between inputs. (2) Handle variable-length inputs. (3) Use the same function at each time step (parameter sharing across time steps).

**Q69: How does parameter sharing in RNNs make the model length-independent?**

> **A:** Since the same weights (U, W, V) are used at every time step, the model computes the same function regardless of how many steps the sequence runs. Adding more time steps doesn't require any new parameters — you just run the same function more times.

**Q70: Write the hidden state update equation for an RNN and explain each term.**

> **A:** H_i = tanh(X_i·U + H_{i-1}·W + b). X_i is the current input, multiplied by input-hidden weights U. H_{i-1} is the previous hidden state (memory), multiplied by recurrent weights W. b is a bias. The tanh squashes the result to [-1, 1].

**Q71: Explain the Many-to-Many RNN architecture for machine translation.**

> **A:** An Encoder RNN reads the source sentence word by word, producing a fixed-length context vector. A Decoder RNN takes this context vector and generates the target sentence word by word until an end-of-sequence token. This handles different input and output sequence lengths.

**Q72: Why can't a simple feedforward network (where all previous inputs are concatenated) solve the sequence problem?**

> **A:** At time step i, you would feed x_1, x_2, ..., x_i as input — so the input size grows with each step, requiring a different network at each time step. This means a 10-word sentence needs 10 different networks, a 100-word sentence needs 100. This is not scalable and violates the requirement for a fixed, reusable function.

## 17. BPTT, Vanishing & Exploding Gradients

### Backpropagation Through Time (BPTT)

RNNs are trained using **BPTT** — standard backpropagation applied to the unrolled computational graph over time.

**Total loss** = sum of losses at each time step:

$$L(\theta) = \sum_t L_t(\theta)$$

**Gradient with respect to V** (output weights): straightforward — depends only on the current time step's output.

**Gradient with respect to W** (recurrent weights): complex — W affects ALL previous hidden states. The gradient must sum over ALL paths from the loss back through time:

$$\frac{\partial L_t}{\partial W} = \sum_{k=1}^{t} \frac{\partial L_t}{\partial s_t} \cdot \frac{\partial s_t}{\partial s_k} \cdot \frac{\partial s_k}{\partial W}$$

The term ∂s_t/∂s_k is a product of t-k Jacobian matrices — this is the source of vanishing/exploding gradient problems.

### Vanishing Gradients

**Cause:** tanh'(x) ∈ (0, 1). Multiplying many values < 1 together makes the gradient vanish exponentially as we go back through time.

**Consequence:** The RNN cannot learn long-term dependencies — gradients from distant time steps are essentially zero, making those weights' updates negligible.

**Math:** If W eigenvalues < 1 and tanh derivative < 1, the product shrinks to near zero over many steps.

### Exploding Gradients

**Cause:** If the weight matrix W has large eigenvalues, repeated multiplication makes gradients grow exponentially.

**Consequence:** Training becomes unstable — loss diverges, NaN values appear.

### Solutions

|Problem|Solution|Description|
|---|---|---|
|Vanishing|**Truncated BPTT (TBPTT)**|Limit backpropagation to k time steps (skip connection strategy)|
|Exploding|**Gradient Clipping**|Normalize gradients to stay within a threshold (e.g., [-0.7, 0.7])|
|Both|**LSTM / GRU**|Gating mechanisms control gradient flow, designed to address both problems|

#### Truncated BPTT (TBPTT)

- Update weights every k₁ steps, backpropagating back only k₂ steps
- For a 1,000-step sequence: split into 50 sequences of 20 steps each
- **Limitation:** Blind to dependencies spanning > k₂ steps

#### Gradient Clipping

If ‖g‖ > threshold: g ← g × threshold/‖g‖

---

### 📝 Q&A — Topic 17

**Q73: Why does BPTT involve multiplying many Jacobian matrices?**

> **A:** In a recurrent network, the hidden state at time t depends on the hidden state at t-1, which depends on t-2, etc. Computing ∂L/∂W requires summing over all paths from the loss back to each time step, and each path involves multiplying the Jacobians of the hidden state transitions: ∂s_t/∂s_k = ∏_{j=k}^{t-1} ∂s_{j+1}/∂s_j.

**Q74: Explain why vanishing gradients cause RNNs to "forget" long-term context.**

> **A:** The gradient signal from distant time steps shrinks exponentially as it is multiplied through many tanh derivatives (each < 1). By the time the gradient reaches early time steps, it is effectively zero. The weights that handle early inputs receive almost no update signal, so the model cannot learn to use distant context.

**Q75: How does gradient clipping prevent exploding gradients?**

> **A:** If the gradient norm exceeds a threshold, the gradient vector is rescaled so its norm equals the threshold. This keeps the weight update in a bounded range, preventing the loss from diverging.

**Q76: What is the key difference between TBPTT and full BPTT?**

> **A:** Full BPTT backpropagates through the entire sequence. TBPTT applies the weight update every k₁ steps, backpropagating only k₂ steps back. This is cheaper and prevents gradients from vanishing/exploding over very long sequences, but it cannot capture dependencies longer than k₂ steps.

## 18. LSTM

### What LSTM Adds Over Vanilla RNN

|Feature|Vanilla RNN|LSTM|
|---|---|---|
|Memory|Hidden state only (fades quickly)|Cell state (persistent) + hidden state|
|Gradient flow|Vanishes over long sequences|Controlled by gates (prevents vanishing)|
|Gates|None|Forget, Input, Output|

### The Core Idea: Cell State and Hidden State

- **Cell state (c_t):** The "long-term memory" conveyor belt. Changes slowly, controlled by multiplicative gates.
- **Hidden state (h_t):** The "short-term working memory." Can change quickly between steps.

$$c \text{ changes slowly} \quad h \text{ changes faster}$$

### The Four Gates

#### 1. Forget Gate

> "What from the old cell state should I forget?"

$$f_t = \sigma(W_f \cdot [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_f)$$

- Looks at input x_t and previous hidden state h_{t-1}
- Output ∈ (0, 1) per cell
- 0 = completely forget; 1 = completely keep

#### 2. Input Gate + Candidate Cell State

> "What new information should I store?"

$$i_t = \sigma(W_i \cdot [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_i) \quad \text{(what to update)}$$

$$\tilde{C}_t = \tanh(W_C \cdot [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_C) \quad \text{(new candidate values)}$$

#### 3. New Cell State Update

> "Update the memory based on forget and input decisions."

$$C_t = f_t \times C_{t-1} + i_t \times \tilde{C}_t$$

- $f_t \times C_{t-1}$: forget what we decided to forget
- $i_t \times \tilde{C}_t$: add the new information

#### 4. Output Gate

> "What from the cell state should I output as the hidden state?"

$$o_t = \sigma(W_o \cdot [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_o)$$

$$h_t = o_t \times \tanh(C_t)$$

First filter what to output (sigmoid), then squash the cell state (tanh), then combine.

### Why tanh and sigmoid are Used

|Activation|Range|Role|
|---|---|---|
|**tanh**|(-1, 1)|Regulates values flowing through the network; derivative stays reasonable → prevents exploding gradients|
|**sigmoid**|(0, 1)|Gate controller — 0 = forget completely, 1 = keep completely|

---

### 📝 Q&A — Topic 18

**Q77: What is the fundamental innovation of LSTM over vanilla RNN?**

> **A:** LSTM introduces a separate cell state vector that acts as long-term memory, plus three gates (forget, input, output) that learn to selectively read, write, and erase information. This controlled gradient flow prevents vanishing gradients and enables learning of very long-range dependencies.

**Q78: What does the forget gate do, and why is it important?**

> **A:** The forget gate computes f_t = σ(...) ∈ (0,1) and multiplies the old cell state by it: f_t × C_{t-1}. Values near 0 erase old memory; values near 1 preserve it. Without a forget gate, the cell state would accumulate information indefinitely.

**Q79: Why does the cell state use addition while the hidden state uses multiplication?**

> **A:** The cell state update C_t = f_t × C_{t-1} + i_t × C̃_t adds a new term to the existing state. This additive pathway means the gradient can flow back through time without multiplicative attenuation — a key reason LSTM avoids vanishing gradients. The hidden state h_t = o_t × tanh(C_t) uses multiplication to selectively expose parts of the cell state.

**Q80: Explain the difference between the cell state and hidden state in LSTM.**

> **A:** The cell state C_t is the long-term memory — it changes slowly and selectively (only gates allow modifications). The hidden state h_t is the short-term output — it can change dramatically between steps and is what gets passed to the next layer or used as output. The cell state is internal; the hidden state is both passed to the next time step and used as the output.

**Q81: How does sigmoid activation function as a gate?**

> **A:** Sigmoid outputs values in (0, 1). When a sigmoid output is multiplied with a vector: a value of 0 completely blocks the information (forget); a value of 1 passes it through unchanged (keep). The network learns to use sigmoid gates to dynamically decide what to remember and what to discard.

## 19. GRU

### GRU vs. LSTM vs. RNN

|Property|Vanilla RNN|LSTM|GRU|
|---|---|---|---|
|Gates|0|3 (forget, input, output)|2 (reset, update)|
|States|1 (hidden)|2 (cell + hidden)|1 (hidden)|
|Parameters|Fewest|Most|Fewer than LSTM|
|Computation|Fastest|Slowest|Faster than LSTM|
|Performance on complex tasks|Worst|Often best|Comparable to LSTM|
|Performance on simple/small data|OK|May overfit|Often better than LSTM|

### GRU Architecture

GRU replaces the cell state and separate gates with a unified hidden state controlled by two gates:

#### Step 1: Compute Gates

**Reset gate** (what to forget from previous hidden state): $$r_t = \sigma(W_r \cdot [h_{t-1}, x_t])$$

**Update gate** (how much of old hidden state to keep): $$z_t = \sigma(W_z \cdot [h_{t-1}, x_t])$$

#### Step 2: Candidate Activation Vector

$$\tilde{h}_t = \tanh(W \cdot [r_t \odot h_{t-1}, x_t])$$

The reset gate r_t determines how much of the previous hidden state influences the candidate.

#### Step 3: New Hidden State

$$h_t = (1 - z_t) \odot h_{t-1} + z_t \odot \tilde{h}_t$$

The update gate z_t controls how much of the old state to retain vs. how much new candidate to incorporate.

### Applications of GRU

- Language modeling
- Machine translation
- Speech recognition
- Time series prediction

---

### 📝 Q&A — Topic 19

**Q82: What is the reset gate in GRU and how does it relate to LSTM's forget gate?**

> **A:** The reset gate r_t ∈ (0,1) determines how much of the previous hidden state h_{t-1} to use when computing the candidate activation. Near 0 = ignore past; near 1 = use past fully. It is analogous to LSTM's forget gate but applied within the candidate computation rather than to a separate cell state.

**Q83: Why does GRU have fewer parameters than LSTM?**

> **A:** LSTM has 4 weight matrices (for forget, input, candidate, and output gates) and maintains 2 separate state vectors. GRU has only 2 gate weight matrices and 1 state vector, resulting in ~25% fewer parameters. This makes GRU faster to train, especially on smaller datasets.

**Q84: What does the update gate do and why is the formula h_t = (1-z_t)·h_{t-1} + z_t·h̃_t elegant?**

> **A:** z_t ∈ (0,1) interpolates between the old hidden state h_{t-1} and the new candidate h̃_t. When z_t → 0, the hidden state barely changes (retains old memory). When z_t → 1, the state fully updates to the new candidate. The formula is elegant because it ensures the interpolation weights sum to 1, making it a convex combination.

**Q85: When would you prefer GRU over LSTM in practice?**

> **A:** GRU is preferred when: the dataset is small (fewer parameters helps with generalization), computational resources are limited (faster training), or the task is relatively simple. LSTM is preferred for complex tasks with long-range dependencies and when sufficient data is available.

## 20. Advanced Sequence Architectures

### Bidirectional RNNs (BiRNN/BiLSTM)

**Problem:** Standard RNNs only have access to **past context**. Many tasks require future context too.

**Solution:** Process the sequence in both directions simultaneously:

- **Forward pass:** t = 1 to T
- **Backward pass:** t = T to 1
- **Combine** both directions' outputs at each position

Used for: POS tagging, NER, question answering — anywhere full context is needed.

### Encoder-Decoder Architecture

**For variable-length input → variable-length output tasks (e.g., translation):**

```
Encoder RNN/LSTM:
French: [Comment] [allez] [vous] → [Context Vector]

Decoder RNN/LSTM:
[Context Vector] → [How] [are] [you] [<END>]
```

1. **Encoder:** Reads the source sequence; produces a fixed-length context vector
2. **Decoder:** A "conditional language model" — generates the target sequence conditioned on the context vector

The decoder finds the sentence that maximizes:

$$P(y_1, y_2, \ldots, y_m | x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n)$$

### Teacher Forcing vs. Free Running

|Strategy|Training Input|Pros|Cons|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Teacher Forcing**|Correct ground-truth tokens|Fast convergence|Exposure bias (mismatch at inference)|
|**Free Running**|Model's own previous output|Trains as inference|Much harder to learn|
|**Scheduled Sampling**|Mixture: GT early, model output later|Best of both|Hyperparameter tuning needed|

**Exposure Bias:** During training with teacher forcing, the model never learns to recover from its own mistakes. At inference, it must use its own (possibly wrong) outputs as inputs — performance can degrade.

### Beam Search

**Problem:** Finding the maximum probability output sequence is NP-hard (exponential search space).

**Greedy search:** Always pick the highest-probability next token. Simple but gets stuck in **local minima**.

**Beam Search:**

1. Maintain **B** (beam width) candidate sequences
2. At each step, expand all B candidates by all vocabulary words
3. Keep only the top-B scoring (log-probability) sequences
4. Continue until all B sequences end with `<EOS>`

**Key formula (with length normalization):**

$$\text{Score} = \frac{1}{|Y|^\alpha} \sum_t \log P(y_t | y_{<t}, x)$$

Length normalization prevents bias toward short sequences. α is a tunable penalty.

- Beam width B=1 → greedy search
- Larger B → better output but more computation

### Temperature Scaling

**Temperature T** controls the sharpness of the softmax distribution:

$$P(y) = \text{softmax}(\text{logits} / T)$$

|T|Effect|Use Case|
|---|---|---|
|T > 1|Flatter distribution (more uniform)|More diverse/creative generation|
|T = 1|Standard softmax|Neutral|
|T < 1|Sharper distribution (more confident)|More focused/predictable generation|

### Auto-regressive Models

Models where each new token is generated conditioned on all previously generated tokens:

$$P(w_1, w_2, \ldots, w_n) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} P(w_i | w_1, \ldots, w_{i-1})$$

This is used in: GPT, language model decoders, sequence-to-sequence systems.

---

### 📝 Q&A — Topic 20

**Q86: What is the exposure bias problem and how does Scheduled Sampling address it?**

> **A:** Teacher forcing trains the model on ground-truth inputs, but at inference the model must use its own (possibly wrong) previous outputs. This mismatch (exposure bias) causes compounding errors. Scheduled Sampling addresses this by gradually increasing the proportion of model-generated inputs during training, helping the model learn to recover from its own mistakes.

**Q87: Why does beam search with B=1 reduce to greedy search?**

> **A:** With beam width B=1, only one candidate sequence is maintained at each step. Expanding with all vocabulary words and keeping only the top-1 is exactly the greedy decision — always pick the single highest-scoring next token.

**Q88: Why is length normalization needed in beam search scoring?**

> **A:** The score is a sum of log-probabilities. Longer sequences accumulate more terms, making their total log-probability lower than shorter ones — even if the longer sequence is better. Dividing by the sequence length (or a length penalty) removes this bias.

**Q89: What does a temperature of T > 1 do to the generation process?**

> **A:** Dividing logits by T > 1 reduces their magnitude before softmax, flattening the probability distribution. This makes lower-probability tokens more likely to be sampled, increasing diversity and creativity in generation. T < 1 does the opposite — makes the model more confident and predictable.

**Q90: What is the role of the context vector in Encoder-Decoder architecture?**

> **A:** The context vector is the fixed-length representation produced by the encoder after reading the entire source sequence. It encodes all the information the decoder needs to generate the target sequence. The decoder conditions every generated token on this vector, making the generation "conditional" on the source.

## 21. Transfer Learning

### Motivation

Training a model from scratch for every task requires massive amounts of labeled data and compute. **Transfer learning** reuses knowledge gained from a source task to improve learning in a target task.

> **Key Insight:** If a bicycle rider learns to ride a motorbike faster, knowledge transferred. If they try to learn piano, knowledge does NOT transfer.

### Formal Definitions (Pan & Yang, 2009)

**Domain D** = {Feature Space X, Marginal Distribution P(X)}

**Task T** = {Label Space Y, Prior P(Y), Conditional Distribution P(Y|X)}

**Transfer Learning Goal:** Given source domain D_S and task T_S, learn the target P_T(Y_T|X_T) in domain D_T using knowledge from D_S and T_S where **D_S ≠ D_T or T_S ≠ T_T**.

### Transfer Learning Scenarios

|Scenario|What differs|Name|
|---|---|---|
|P_S(Y_S) ≠ P_T(Y_T)|Label distributions differ|Domain Adaptation|
|P_S(Y_S|X_S) ≠ P_T(Y_T|X_T)|
|Y_S ≠ Y_T|Label spaces differ → Sequential = Sequential TL; Simultaneous = MTL||
|P_S(X_S) ≠ P_T(X_T)|Marginal distributions differ|Domain Adaptation|
|X_S ≠ X_T|Feature spaces differ (different languages)|Cross-lingual Learning|

### Types of Transfer Learning

|Type|Label availability|Source/Target Tasks|
|---|---|---|
|**Transductive**|Labels only from source domain|Source task = Target task|
|**Inductive**|Labels available in target domain|Source ≠ Target task|
|**Unsupervised**|No labels in either domain|Word embeddings (one corpus → another)|
|**Homogeneous**|X_S = X_T, Y_S = Y_T|Same feature and label spaces|
|**Heterogeneous**|X_S ≠ X_T or Y_S ≠ Y_T|Different spaces|

### Transfer Learning Approaches

#### 1. Feature-Based Transfer

Extract robust features from the source domain and apply them to the target. Assumes good features generalize across domains.

**Feature Extraction (Frozen):** Keep all pre-trained weights frozen; train only new classifier layers. Best when target data is small and similar to source.

#### 2. Instance-Based Transfer

Select and re-weight instances from the source domain to reduce distribution mismatch.

- **TrAdaBoost:** Reduces the weight of source instances that are harmful for the target task
- Example: Using historical attack data to train an intrusion detection system on new attacks

#### 3. Relation-Based Transfer

Transfer logical relationships and structural rules from source to target.

- Example: Learning subject-verb-object relations in English and applying to Hindi

#### 4. Parameter-Based Transfer (Fine-Tuning)

Share model parameters between source and target models.

|Fine-Tuning Type|Description|
|---|---|
|**Feature Extraction (Frozen)**|All pre-trained weights frozen; only new layers trained|
|**Full Fine-Tuning**|All weights updated with low learning rates|
|**Partial Fine-Tuning**|Freeze early layers; fine-tune later layers|
|**Discriminative Fine-Tuning**|Different learning rates per layer (earlier = smaller LR)|

**Typical learning rates:**

- Early layers (universal features): 1e-5
- Middle layers: 1e-4
- Final layers (task-specific): 1e-3

### Negative Transfer

Occurs when the source knowledge **hurts** rather than helps learning in the target domain. Happens when domains have little in common. A key challenge in transfer learning.

### When to Use Which Approach

**Feature Extraction when:**

- Target dataset < 1,000 samples per class
- High domain similarity
- Limited computational resources
- Rapid prototyping

**Full Transfer Learning when:**

- Domains differ significantly
- Need to understand transferable representations
- Domain-specific architecture needed

### 📝 Q&A — Topic 21

**Q91: What is the formal definition of transfer learning (Pan & Yang)?**

> **A:** Given source domain D_S and source task T_S, and target domain D_T and target task T_T, transfer learning learns P_T(Y_T|X_T) using knowledge from D_S and T_S, where D_S ≠ D_T or T_S ≠ T_T. Either the domain (feature distribution) or the task (label space/distribution) must differ.

**Q92: What is the difference between transductive and inductive transfer learning?**

> **A:** In transductive TL, the source and target tasks are the same (same label space), so labels only come from the source domain — the target domain has no labels. In inductive TL, the source and target tasks differ, and labeled data is available in the target domain.

**Q93: What is negative transfer and when does it occur?**

> **A:** Negative transfer occurs when knowledge from the source domain hurts performance on the target task. It happens when source and target domains are too dissimilar, causing the model to apply irrelevant or conflicting patterns. Example: knowledge from bicycle riding cannot help piano playing.

**Q94: What is discriminative fine-tuning and why is it used?**

> **A:** Discriminative fine-tuning applies different learning rates to different layers — smaller rates for early layers (which capture universal features that should not change much) and larger rates for later layers (which need more adaptation for the specific task). This preserves general features while adapting task-specific ones.

**Q95: What is cross-lingual transfer learning?**

> **A:** It refers to training a model on a high-resource language (e.g., English) and applying it to a low-resource language (e.g., Hindi, Telugu) where the feature space X_S ≠ X_T (different scripts/vocabulary). The model transfers structural knowledge (syntax, semantics) across languages.

## 22. Multitask Learning

### Definition

**Multitask Learning (MTL)** trains a single model on multiple tasks simultaneously.

> "MTL improves generalization by leveraging the domain-specific information contained in the training signals of related tasks."

- All tasks are learned **in parallel** (vs. Sequential Transfer Learning)
- A type of **inductive transfer learning**
- The inductive bias from auxiliary tasks causes the model to prefer hypotheses that explain multiple tasks

### Why MTL Works

For tasks A and B sharing hidden representation F:

1. **Implicit Data Augmentation:** Effectively increases the dataset — reduces overfitting to any one task
2. **Attention Focusing:** With multiple tasks, the model focuses on features that matter across tasks (noise from one task's data is countered by signal from others)
3. **Representation Bias:** MTL biases the model toward representations that multiple tasks prefer
4. **Regularization:** MTL acts as a regularizer — reduces risk of overfitting

### Hard Parameter Sharing

All tasks share the **same hidden layers**, with separate output heads per task.

```
Input → [Shared BERT/LSTM Layers] → Task 1 Output Head
                                  → Task 2 Output Head
                                  → Task 3 Output Head
```

- Greatly reduces overfitting risk
- The more tasks, the more the model must learn universal representations
- Risk of shared parameter overfitting decreases at rate O(N) where N = number of tasks

### Soft Parameter Sharing

Each task has its **own model and parameters**, but the distance between parameters is **regularized** to encourage similarity:

$$L = L_1 + L_2 + \lambda |W_1 - W_2|^2$$

This allows tasks to differ more freely while still benefiting from each other.

### Challenges: Negative Transfer in MTL

When task objectives **interfere with each other**:

- Tasks may need to be learned at different rates
- One task may dominate the learning
- Gradients from different tasks may conflict, making optimization harder

When tasks are genuinely related: performance on both tasks improves. When they are unrelated: negative transfer.

### Hierarchical MTL

For tasks with **hierarchical labels** (e.g., offensive language with subtasks A→B→C), each subproblem can be an MTL task, with shared BERT layers and task-specific output heads for each level.

### MTL + Ensemble

An ensemble approach:

1. Train CNN, LSTM, and GRU separately in a multitask setup for related tasks
2. Extract intermediate shared layer representations from all three
3. Concatenate with handcrafted feature vectors (TF-IDF, lexicon-based)
4. Feed into a final MLP with both shared and task-specific layers

---

### 📝 Q&A — Topic 22

**Q96: What is the key difference between hard and soft parameter sharing in MTL?**

> **A:** Hard parameter sharing physically shares the same weights across all tasks in a common hidden layer. Soft parameter sharing gives each task its own weights but penalizes (via regularization) large differences between weights, encouraging them to be similar without forcing identity.

**Q97: Explain how MTL acts as an implicit regularizer.**

> **A:** With N tasks, the model must find a representation that works for all of them. This constraint prevents overfitting to the specific noise or biases of any single task's training data. The more tasks, the stronger the regularization effect — the shared parameters cannot memorize task-specific noise.

**Q98: What causes negative transfer in MTL and how is it different from negative transfer in sequential TL?**

> **A:** In MTL, negative transfer occurs when task gradients conflict — updating weights to improve task A degrades task B, because the tasks' loss surfaces are incompatible. In sequential TL, negative transfer occurs because the pre-trained source knowledge is irrelevant or harmful for the target domain.

**Q99: Why might training tasks with hierarchical labels benefit from MTL?**

> **A:** Hierarchical labels have natural dependencies — e.g., you can only categorize an offense (Task B) if it IS offensive (Task A). Training all subtasks together forces the shared layers to learn features relevant across all levels of the hierarchy, and each task provides auxiliary signal that helps the others.

## 23. Continual / Lifelong Learning

### Definition

**Continual learning:** Continuously updating a model to adapt to new tasks or data distributions **without catastrophically forgetting** previously learned information.

**Lifelong learning:** Extends continual learning over the model's entire operational lifespan — accumulating knowledge like a human does throughout their life.

### The Core Challenge: Catastrophic Forgetting

When a neural network learns task B, the weight updates for B can overwrite the weights learned for task A, destroying performance on A. This is called **catastrophic forgetting**.

### Stability-Plasticity Tradeoff

|Property|Definition|
|---|---|
|**Stability**|Keep weights unchanged to not forget old tasks|
|**Plasticity**|Allow weight changes to learn new tasks|

These are in direct tension — the challenge is finding the right balance.

---

### Strategy 1: Rehearsal-Based Methods

Maintain a **small memory buffer** of examples from old tasks. When learning a new task, replay stored samples alongside new data.

Two forms:

1. **Store representative samples** from old tasks directly
2. **Generative replay:** Train a GAN/VAE to generate synthetic old-task data when needed

---

### Strategy 2: Architecture-Based Methods

Dedicate specific parts of the network to specific tasks; never modify task-specific regions.

**Class Incremental (CIL):** Add new output neurons for new classes. Challenge: the model must correctly route test samples to the right output head without knowing which task they belong to.

**Domain Incremental (DIL):** Output layer stays fixed unless entirely new classes appear. Task identity is available at train time but NOT at test time.

**Task Incremental (TIL) / Multi-Head:** Separate output layer per task (like MTL). Task identity IS available at test time.

**Progressive Neural Networks (PNN):**

- Train Task 1 → freeze its column
- For Task 2, add a new column with lateral connections to Task 1's hidden layers
- Task 2 can selectively use Task 1's representations without modifying them
- Avoids forgetting entirely; enables forward transfer; but grows in size with each task

---

### Strategy 3: Regularization-Based Methods

Identify important weights for old tasks and penalize changes to those weights.

**Elastic Weight Consolidation (EWC):**

$$L(\theta) = L_B(\theta) + \sum_i \frac{\lambda}{2} F_i (\theta_i - \theta_{A,i}^*)^2$$

Where:

- L_B = loss on new task B
- F_i = Fisher information (measures importance of weight i for old task A)
- θ_{A,i}* = optimal weights for task A
- λ = regularization strength

The **Fisher Information Matrix** approximates how sensitive the loss is to each weight — weights that mattered most for old tasks are penalized heavily for changing.

Intuition: EWC finds a solution for Task B that doesn't stray too far from the Task A optimal weights, weighted by how important each weight was.

### 📝 Q&A — Topic 23

**Q100: What is catastrophic forgetting and why does it happen?**

> **A:** Catastrophic forgetting occurs when training a neural network on a new task overwrites the weights that were optimized for a previous task. Since all tasks share the same weights, gradient updates for Task B can erase the patterns learned for Task A, causing performance on A to collapse.

**Q101: Explain the stability-plasticity tradeoff.**

> **A:** Stability means keeping weights fixed to preserve old knowledge (no forgetting). Plasticity means allowing weights to change to learn new things. A perfectly stable system cannot learn anything new; a perfectly plastic system forgets everything immediately. Continual learning algorithms try to find the right balance.

**Q102: What is the key insight behind Elastic Weight Consolidation (EWC)?**

> **A:** Not all weights are equally important to an old task. EWC uses the Fisher Information Matrix to estimate how sensitive the old task's loss is to each weight. Weights with high importance are penalized strongly for changing; unimportant weights are free to adapt. This allows the network to learn new tasks while protecting the core knowledge of old ones.

**Q103: How do Progressive Neural Networks avoid catastrophic forgetting?**

> **A:** PNNs freeze previously trained task columns entirely — their weights are never modified. New tasks get new columns that can access old columns' representations through lateral connections. Since old weights are frozen, they cannot be overwritten. The cost is that the network grows with each new task.

**Q104: What is generative replay in rehearsal-based continual learning?**

> **A:** Instead of storing actual samples from old tasks (which may be privacy-sensitive or storage-intensive), a generative model (GAN or VAE) is trained to produce synthetic examples resembling old-task data. When learning a new task, the model is also trained on these generated pseudo-examples, simulating "remembering" the old tasks.

**Q105: What is the difference between Class Incremental, Domain Incremental, and Task Incremental learning?**

> **A:** CIL adds new output neurons as new classes are encountered; the model must identify which class without knowing which task. DIL keeps the output size fixed (unless truly new classes appear) and task identity is NOT available at test time. TIL has a separate output head per task, and task identity IS known at test time — closest to standard MTL but sequential.
