# 1. WHAT IS RAG?

## Background — Issues with LLMs

Large Language Models (LLMs) like GPT-4, Claude, and Gemini have shown remarkable ability in generating human-like text, but they suffer from two critical problems:

- **Factual Inaccuracies**: LLMs rely solely on _parametric knowledge_ (knowledge baked into their weights during training). They can confidently produce wrong information.
- **Lack of Up-to-Date Information**: Once trained, an LLM cannot access new information without full retraining. Events after the training cutoff are unknown to the model.

These problems led to the development of **Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)**.

## Definition

**RAG** is the technique of:

1. **Retrieving** relevant information from an _external data source_ (outside the LLM)
2. **Augmenting** the LLM's input prompt with that external information
3. **Generating** a more accurate, grounded response

> RAG combines the strengths of **Information Retrieval (IR)** and **Natural Language Generation (NLG)**.

## The Three Steps of RAG

|Step|What Happens|
|---|---|
|Retrieve|Fetch relevant documents/chunks from an external knowledge base|
|Augment|Combine the retrieved information with the user query into a prompt|
|Generate|LLM produces a grounded, accurate answer using the augmented prompt|

## Parametric vs Non-Parametric Memory

|Type|Description|Example|
|---|---|---|
|Parametric Memory|Knowledge stored in the model's weights (learned during training)|GPT-4 has ~1.76 trillion parameters; LLaMA 3 largest has 80B parameters|
|Non-Parametric Memory|External, changeable knowledge base not baked into weights|A vector database of company documents|

RAG is a way of giving LLMs **non-parametric memory**, coined by Lewis et al. in their 2020 paper "Retrieval-Augmented Generation for Knowledge-Intensive NLP Tasks" (arXiv:2005.11401).

## Why Not Pure Retrieval or Pure Generation?

**Pure Retrieval Models:**

- Rely on pre-existing fixed responses — can become repetitive
- Cannot deeply understand conversation context
- Cannot disambiguate context

**Pure Generative Models (NLG):**

- May lack access to specific, factual information
- Without a good dataset, can generate generic or nonsensical responses
- Do not have nuanced understanding of context

**RAG combines both to overcome these weaknesses.**

## RAG Use Cases

- **Search Engines**: Google-style search with LLM-generated summaries and source citations
- **Personalized Marketing Content**: Real-time trend-aware blogs, emails, social media posts (e.g., Yarnit, Jasper, Simplified)
- **Real-Time Event Commentary**: Connecting to live APIs for sports/news commentary (e.g., IBM at 2023 US Open Tennis)
- **Document Question Answering**: Querying proprietary, non-public company documents
- **Virtual Assistants**: Siri, Alexa augmented with user-specific context
- **Conversational Agents / Chatbots**: Customer support bots using product/service manuals

# 2. NAÏVE RAG

## Overview

**Naïve RAG** (also called "Traditional RAG") is a **sequential Retrieve-and-Read** process:

```
User Query → Retrieve Documents → Augment Prompt → LLM Generates Answer
```

It is a linear, single-pass pipeline with no self-correction or dynamic decision making. The system retrieves documents based on the query, dumps them into the prompt, and generates a response — no questions asked about quality.

---

### Q&A — Section 2: Naïve RAG

**Q1. What does "sequential Retrieve and Read" mean in Naïve RAG?** A1. It means the process is strictly linear and single-pass: retrieve documents first, then read/generate from them, with no loops, re-evaluation, or correction steps.

**Q2. Why is Naïve RAG sometimes described as having a "garbage in, garbage out" problem?** A2. Because it does not check the quality or relevance of retrieved documents before feeding them to the LLM. If retrieval returns poor or irrelevant documents, the generation will also be poor, yet the LLM will still produce a confident-sounding answer.

# 3. ISSUES & DRAWBACKS OF NAÏVE RAG

## Retrieval Problems

- **Low Recall**: Relevant documents may not be retrieved at all
- **Low Precision**: Irrelevant documents are retrieved along with relevant ones

## Augmentation Problems

- Documents retrieved from different sources may have **different or conflicting contexts**
- The **LLM's context window** may not be large enough to accommodate all retrieved documents

## Generation Problems

- Due to the upstream weaknesses above, the generation suffers from:
    - **Hallucination**: LLM fabricates information
    - **Lack of Groundedness**: Generated content is not properly anchored in the retrieved context

## Broader Systemic Issues

|Issue|Description|
|---|---|
|Information Overload|RAG retrieves for every query even when retrieval is not needed, forcing the LLM to process large amounts of potentially irrelevant data|
|Irrelevant Data Incorporation|Not all retrieved information is relevant; incorporating it reduces quality and accuracy|
|Lack of Discretion|No built-in mechanism to decide _when_ retrieval is actually necessary; wastes computational resources on simple queries|

---

### Q&A — Section 3: Issues with Naïve RAG

**Q1. List three major drawbacks of Naïve RAG, one each for retrieval, augmentation, and generation.** A1. Retrieval: Low precision and recall. Augmentation: Conflicting contexts from different sources may overflow the context window. Generation: Hallucinations and lack of groundedness due to poor upstream data.

**Q2. What is the "Information Overload" problem in Naïve RAG?** A2. The system retrieves information for every query indiscriminately, forcing the LLM to process large amounts of potentially irrelevant data, degrading response quality and wasting computation.

**Q3. What does "Lack of Discretion" mean in the context of Naïve RAG?** A3. Naïve RAG has no mechanism to judge whether a given query actually requires external retrieval. Simple factual questions that the LLM already knows well are still sent through the retrieval pipeline unnecessarily.

# 4. ADVANCED RAG — OVERVIEW

## The Core Framework

Advanced RAG replaces Naïve RAG's simple Retrieve-and-Read with a richer framework:

```
Rewrite → Retrieve → Re-rank → Read
```

This is often called the **4R Framework**.

## Three Areas of Improvement

|Area|Focus|
|---|---|
|Pre-Retrieval Strategies|Optimize the indexing structure and the original query before retrieval even begins|
|Retrieval Strategies|Improve the retrieval effectiveness itself — better precision, recall, relevance|
|Post-Retrieval Strategies|Rerank and compress the retrieved chunks for better LLM consumption|

---

### Q&A — Section 4: Advanced RAG Overview

**Q1. What is the "4R Framework" of Advanced RAG?** A1. Rewrite (query optimization), Retrieve (improved retrieval), Re-rank (reorder results by relevance), Read (generate the final answer).

**Q2. Into what three broad categories do Advanced RAG improvements fall?** A2. Pre-retrieval strategies, Retrieval strategies, and Post-retrieval strategies.

# 5. ADVANCED RAG — PRE-RETRIEVAL TECHNIQUES

Pre-retrieval focuses on making the documents and queries as retrieval-ready as possible _before_ any search takes place.

## 5.1 Index Optimization

The goal is to improve how documents are stored so they are more searchable and relevant.

### Chunk Optimization

- **Balance chunk sizes**: Too large → loses focus; too small → loses context.
- Must strike a balance between precision (small chunks) and coherence (large chunks).

### Context-Enriched Chunking

- Add a **summary** to each chunk to improve retrieval accuracy.
- A retriever can match on the summary rather than the raw text.

### Metadata Enhancements

- Add tags and metadata to chunks: timestamps, categories, document origin, author, etc.
- Metadata is invaluable for **filtering** during retrieval (e.g., "only look at documents from 2023").
- Can also resolve conflicting information by checking chronology or origin.

## 5.2 Query Optimization

The original user query may be poorly phrased, ambiguous, or insufficient for retrieval. Query optimization improves it before sending it to the retriever.

- **Query Expansion**: Add synonyms or related terms to the query
- **Query Rewriting**: Use an LLM to rephrase the query into a cleaner, more retrieval-friendly form

---

### Q&A — Section 5: Pre-Retrieval Techniques

**Q1. Why is chunk size balance important in RAG?** A1. Too-large chunks introduce noise and irrelevant information. Too-small chunks lose contextual coherence and broader themes. The optimal chunk size gives the retriever precise, focused, yet complete pieces of information.

**Q2. What is Context-Enriched Chunking?** A2. It involves attaching an LLM-generated summary to each chunk during indexing so that the retriever can match on the summary rather than raw text, improving retrieval accuracy.

**Q3. Why is metadata important in the RAG indexing pipeline?** A3. Metadata (like timestamps, categories, authorship) enables filtering during retrieval, helps resolve conflicting information by checking origin/chronology, and allows the system to narrow the search space efficiently.

**Q4. What is the difference between Query Expansion and Query Rewriting?** A4. Query Expansion adds synonyms or related terms to broaden the query. Query Rewriting uses an LLM to rephrase the original query into a cleaner, more specific, retrieval-friendly form.

# 6. ADVANCED RAG — RETRIEVAL STRATEGIES

## 6.1 Hybrid Retrieval

**Hybrid Retrieval** combines multiple search methods for better results:

- **Keyword-based search** (sparse, like BM25 / TF-IDF)
- **Semantic/dense vector search** (embedding-based)
- **Knowledge graph-based search**

The retrieval can be a **union** (higher recall, lower precision) or an **intersection** (lower recall, higher precision) of all methods, depending on requirements. In practice, a **weighted combination** is most common.

## 6.2 Iterative Retrieval

Instead of a single linear retrieve-generate pass, **Iterative Retrieval** searches the knowledge base **repeatedly** in a loop.

- A **JUDGE** (typically an LLM-as-a-Judge, like GPT-4 or a prompt-engineered smaller model) is the key component.
- The Judge's responsibilities:
    1. **Sufficiency Check**: Determine if current retrieved information is enough to answer the question, or if another retrieval step is needed.
    2. **Relevance Ranking**: Evaluate retrieved documents and rank them to prioritize the most useful context.
- The loop continues until the Judge is satisfied.

## 6.3 Recursive Retrieval

**Recursive Retrieval** goes deeper than Iterative Retrieval:

|Iterative Retrieval|Recursive Retrieval|
|---|---|
|Repeatedly retrieves based on the **same query**|**Updates the query itself** based on previous retrieval steps|
|Refines answers|Explores complex, hierarchical information structures (like graphs)|

- Particularly useful when user needs are not entirely clear or when information is **highly specialized or nuanced**.
- The recursive nature allows **continuous learning and adaptation** to requirements.
- Commonly implemented with the **Chain-of-Thought (CoT)** pattern.
- Often used with a **two-stage approach**:
    1. First: recursive retrieval → generate a summary
    2. Then: use that summary to trigger a **secondary retrieval** (e.g., multi-hop graph retrieval) → final result

## 6.4 Adaptive Retrieval

**Adaptive RAG** uses a **classifier** to select the most appropriate retrieval strategy based on query complexity:

|Strategy|When Used|
|---|---|
|No retrieval|Simple queries the LLM can answer from parametric memory|
|Single-step retrieval|Moderately complex queries|
|Multi-step / iterative retrieval|Complex, multi-faceted queries|

- **Self-RAG** and **FLARE** are the two popular examples of Adaptive Retrieval.
- Adaptive Retrieval is part of the broader trend of **Agentic AI**.

---

### Q&A — Section 6: Retrieval Strategies

**Q1. What is Hybrid Retrieval and what three types of search can it combine?** A1. Hybrid Retrieval combines keyword-based (sparse) search, dense semantic vector search, and knowledge graph-based search. Results are typically combined using a weighted approach.

**Q2. What is the role of the JUDGE in Iterative Retrieval?** A2. The Judge (usually a prompt-engineered LLM like GPT-4) determines whether the currently retrieved information is sufficient to answer the question, and if not, triggers another retrieval step. It also ranks documents by relevance.

**Q3. How does Recursive Retrieval differ from Iterative Retrieval?** A3. Iterative Retrieval repeatedly retrieves using the same query to refine answers. Recursive Retrieval updates the query itself based on each retrieval result to explore deeper into complex, hierarchical information structures.

**Q4. What does an Adaptive RAG classifier decide?** A4. It decides whether a given query requires no retrieval, a single retrieval step, or multi-step/iterative retrieval, thereby optimizing both efficiency and accuracy.

**Q5. Name two popular systems that implement Adaptive Retrieval.** A5. FLARE (Forward-Looking Active REtrieval Augmented Generation) and Self-RAG.

# 7. ADVANCED RAG — POST-RETRIEVAL TECHNIQUES

## Why Post-Retrieval?

Even when retrieval works well, the LLM may still fail because:

- Retrieved chunks may be **redundant or disjointed**
- The LLM **cannot process** all retrieved information due to context window limits
- Irrelevant passages introduce **noise**

Post-retrieval addresses this with two main techniques: **Compression** and **Reranking**.

## 7.1 Compression

**Goal**: Reduce unnecessary context, remove noise, and fit retrieved content within the LLM's context window.

- **Problem**: Excessively long context introduces noise, which diminishes the LLM's ability to extract relevant information → hallucinations and irrelevant responses persist.
- **Solution**: Use a language model to detect and remove **unimportant and irrelevant tokens** (prompt compression).
- The compressed prompt is smaller, focused, and fits within the context window.

## 7.2 Reranking

**Goal**: Reorder retrieved documents so the most relevant ones are at the top (and at the edges of the prompt, which the LLM processes best).

- Refines retrieval results by prioritizing documents that are more **contextually appropriate**.
- Essential when hybrid retrieval is used (resolves priority conflicts between methods).
- Improves overall response quality.
- Common rerankers:
    - **Multi-vector rerankers**
    - **Learning to Rank (LTR)** models
    - **BERT-based rerankers**
    - **Hybrid rerankers**
    - **Cohere Rerank** (specialized API with pre-trained models)

> Key insight: Research shows that LLMs perform better when the most relevant information appears at the **edges** (beginning or end) of the prompt, not in the middle. Reranking exploits this.

---

### Q&A — Section 7: Post-Retrieval Techniques

**Q1. Why is post-retrieval processing needed even if retrieval is accurate?** A1. Because the LLM may still struggle with redundant, disjointed, or excessively long context. Compression reduces noise and fits content within the context window; reranking ensures the most relevant chunks are prioritized.

**Q2. What is Prompt Compression?** A2. Using a language model to detect and remove unimportant/irrelevant tokens from the retrieved context, making the prompt smaller, cleaner, and within the LLM's context window limits.

**Q3. Why does reranking matter specifically for hybrid retrieval?** A3. Hybrid retrieval may return documents from multiple methods (keyword, dense vector, graph) with conflicting priority signals. Reranking provides a unified relevance ordering, resolving these conflicts.

**Q4. Name four types of rerankers mentioned in the slides.** A4. Multi-vector rerankers, Learning to Rank (LTR) models, BERT-based rerankers, and Hybrid rerankers. (Cohere Rerank is a specialized API example.)

# 8. COGNITIVE ARCHITECTURES FOR RAG

The way an RAG system is organized and how it makes decisions constitutes its **cognitive architecture**.

## Three Types of Cognitive Architecture

### Type 1: Chain (Basic)

- The simplest architecture.
- LLM determines what to generate purely based on retrieved documents.
- Linear: query → retrieval → generation. No loops or dynamic routing.

### Type 2: Routing

- An LLM **decides between different retrievers** based on the nature of the question.
- Example: route a factual question to a dense vector database and a keyword question to a BM25 index.

### Type 3: State Machine (Most Powerful)

- Supports **loops and conditional transitions** between steps.
- A state machine defines:
    - A set of **steps** (e.g., retrieval, grade documents, re-write query)
    - **Transition rules** between them (e.g., "if retrieved docs are not relevant → re-write query → re-retrieve")
- Best suited for implementing **CRAG** and **Self-RAG**.

## LangGraph

**LangGraph** is a library by LangChain for implementing LLM state machines.

- Provides flexibility for diverse RAG flows ("flow engineering")
- Supports specific **decision points** (e.g., document grading)
- Supports **loops** (e.g., re-try retrieval)
- Can implement both **CRAG** and **Self-RAG**

---

### Q&A — Section 8: Cognitive Architectures

**Q1. What are the three cognitive architectures for RAG systems?** A1. (1) Chain: simple linear flow; (2) Routing: an LLM routes queries to different retrievers; (3) State Machine: supports loops and conditional transitions between pipeline steps.

**Q2. Why is the State Machine architecture the most powerful for RAG?** A2. Because it allows the system to loop back and retry steps (e.g., re-write a query after poor retrieval results) and make conditional decisions (e.g., only proceed to generation if documents are graded as relevant).

**Q3. What is LangGraph and why is it significant for Advanced RAG?** A3. LangGraph is LangChain's library for implementing LLM state machines. It enables "flow engineering" for RAG with decision points and loops, and can implement both CRAG and Self-RAG.

# 9. CORRECTIVE RAG (C-RAG)

## Motivation

- Basic RAG does not verify retrieved documents. It will synthesize a confident, eloquent answer even from completely irrelevant or wrong documents.
- **C-RAG** is the "bouncer at the door" of the LLM — it adds a **lightweight evaluation step before generation**.

## C-RAG Steps

|Step|Description|
|---|---|
|1. Retrieve|Fetch a set of documents, just like standard RAG|
|2. Evaluate|A small, fast "retrieval evaluator" model assesses each document for relevance/correctness and assigns a **confidence score**|
|3. Triage & Act|Based on confidence scores, take one of three actions|

## The Three Triage Actions

|Situation|Action|
|---|---|
|**Correct** (documents are good)|Proceed to generation; optionally refine to extract key "knowledge strips"|
|**Incorrect** (documents are garbage — below threshold)|Discard all retrieved documents; trigger a **web search** for better, more current information|
|**Ambiguous** (mixed quality)|Use both: refine the good documents AND augment with web search results|

> C-RAG breaks the "garbage in, garbage out" cycle by **verifying before generating**.

## C-RAG Key Characteristics

- **Plug-and-play**: C-RAG is a methodology, not a proprietary model. It can be integrated with any high instruction-following LLM (e.g., Llama 3, GPT-4o).
- Implemented via agentic frameworks:
    - **LangGraph/LangChain**: For corrective logic (routing, document relevance checking, web search decisions)
    - **Ollama**: For running open-source models (Llama 3, Mistral) locally for C-RAG

---

### Q&A — Section 9: C-RAG

**Q1. What core problem does C-RAG solve that standard RAG cannot?** A1. C-RAG solves the problem of generating from poor-quality or irrelevant retrieved documents. Standard RAG does not check document quality; C-RAG adds an evaluation step that can trigger a web search fallback if documents are poor.

**Q2. What are the three triage outcomes in C-RAG and what action follows each?** A2. (1) Correct → proceed to generation. (2) Incorrect → discard documents and trigger web search. (3) Ambiguous → refine good documents and supplement with web search.

**Q3. Is C-RAG tied to a specific LLM?** A3. No. C-RAG is a methodology and is "plug-and-play." Any LLM with strong instruction-following capabilities (such as Llama 3 or GPT-4o) can be used, coupled with a retrieval evaluator component and an agentic framework like LangGraph.

**Q4. What role does LangGraph play in a C-RAG implementation?** A4. LangGraph provides the orchestration framework for the corrective logic: routing queries, checking document relevance, and deciding whether to use the web search fallback.

# 10. FLARE (Forward-Looking Active REtrieval Augmented Generation)

## Core Research Question

> "Can we create a simple and generic retrieval-augmented LM that **actively decides when and what to retrieve** throughout the generation process?"

## Two Core Ideas

### When to Retrieve

- Retrieve **only when the LLM is uncertain**.
- Assumption: LLMs are well-calibrated, meaning token probabilities reflect actual uncertainty.
- If any generated token has **low probability** (below a threshold), the LLM is uncertain → trigger retrieval.
- High-confidence token sequences → no retrieval needed.

### What to Retrieve

- The retrieval query should represent **what the LLM intends to generate next** (forward-looking).
- FLARE **anticipates the future**: generate a temporary "next sentence," use it as a query to retrieve relevant documents, then regenerate the actual next sentence conditioned on the retrieved documents.

## FLARE Step-by-Step Process

1. **Start**: Give the input query to the LLM; get an initial generated output.
2. **Check Confidence**: Inspect token probabilities in the generated output.
    - If all tokens are high-confidence → accept the output, repeat for next portion.
    - If any token has low confidence → trigger a RAG step.
3. **RAG Step**: The query is formed from **only the high-confidence tokens** in the generated sequence (low-confidence tokens are discarded as unreliable for retrieval).
4. **Retrieve**: Perform vector search over the knowledge base to find closest chunks.
5. **Append Context**: Append retrieved chunks to the input as additional context.
6. **Re-generate**: Query the LLM again, this time with the additional context; get the revised sentence.
7. **Append to Input**: Append the revised sentence to the growing input and continue.

> FLARE = active, confidence-triggered, forward-looking retrieval during the generation process.

---

### Q&A — Section 10: FLARE

**Q1. What triggers a retrieval step in FLARE?** A1. A retrieval step is triggered when any token in the LLM's generated output falls below a confidence probability threshold, indicating the LLM is uncertain about that portion of its output.

**Q2. Why does FLARE use only high-confidence tokens as the retrieval query?** A2. High-confidence tokens are what the LLM is sure about and therefore represent reliable, meaningful content for forming a search query. Low-confidence tokens are uncertain and would make for a poor or misleading query.

**Q3. What makes FLARE "forward-looking"?** A3. FLARE generates a temporary anticipated "next sentence" and uses it as a query to retrieve documents needed for future generation steps, rather than looking backward at past content.

**Q4. How does FLARE differ from standard RAG in retrieval triggering?** A4. Standard RAG always retrieves for every query. FLARE retrieves only when token confidence drops below a threshold, making retrieval conditional and adaptive.

# 11. SELF-RAG (SELF-REFLECTIVE RAG)

## Motivation

Traditional RAG pipelines lack adaptability — they are linear and retrieve for every query regardless of need. **Self-RAG** introduces **decision-making capabilities** into the model itself, enabling query refinement and self-assessment.

> Self-RAG led to a **73% improvement in accuracy** over traditional RAG.

## Core Concept: Reflection Tokens

The key innovation of Self-RAG is special tokens called **"reflection tokens"** added to the LLM's vocabulary. These tokens let the model **pause and introspect** about its own generation process.

## The Four Reflection Tokens

|Token|Purpose|Action|
|---|---|---|
|**Retrieve**|Should external information be fetched?|Model assesses whether query can be answered with existing knowledge or needs retrieval|
|**ISREL** (Is Relevant?)|How relevant is the retrieved data?|Filters out irrelevant retrieved information|
|**ISSUP** (Is Supported?)|How well does the model's output align with retrieved data?|Acts as a fact-checking mechanism|
|**ISUSE** (Is Useful?)|How useful is the overall generated response?|Final quality check for helpfulness|

## Four Key Mechanisms of Self-RAG

### 1. Self-Reflection through Reflection Tokens

The model uses the four tokens above to continuously evaluate its own process during generation.

### 2. On-Demand Information Retrieval

- The model first tries to answer using its own parametric knowledge.
- **Only if** it determines external information is needed (by generating a `Retrieve` token) does it trigger retrieval.
- This avoids wasteful retrieval for every query.

### 3. Critical Evaluation of Retrieved Information

- After retrieval, the model uses `ISREL` to assess relevance of each retrieved piece.
- It uses `ISSUP` to determine how well the retrieved data supports the generation.
- Only helpful, accurate data influences the final output.

### 4. Flexible Inference (No Retraining Required)

- Users can **adjust weights assigned to reflection tokens at inference time**.
- Example: increase the weight of `ISSUP` → prioritize outputs strongly supported by evidence.
- This enables task-specific optimization without retraining the whole model.
- Allows the model to adapt to different needs (precision vs. creativity).

## Self-RAG Market Solutions

|Model|Specialization|
|---|---|
|Mistral 7B with Local Contextual Retrieval|Lightweight, optimized for edge devices|
|Meta's LLaMA 3 (Fine-tuned variants)|Domain-specific applications (legal, medical AI)|
|Google Gemini Mini (Self-RAG Mode)|On-device processing, no cloud dependency|
|Anthropic Claude (Memory-Augmented)|Persistent memory for contextual understanding|
|OpenAI GPT-4o|Hybrid self-retrieval + optional external augmentation|

Self-RAG is primarily supported through **fine-tuned open-source models** (Llama-2, Mistral) trained to output specific reflection tokens. It is implemented through **LangGraph**, using high-reasoning models like GPT-4 for the critic component.

---

### Q&A — Section 11: Self-RAG

**Q1. What are "reflection tokens" in Self-RAG and why are they important?** A1. Reflection tokens are special tokens added to the LLM's vocabulary (Retrieve, ISREL, ISSUP, ISUSE) that allow the model to pause, introspect, and make decisions about retrieval and generation quality during its own output process.

**Q2. List the four reflection tokens and the question each answers.** A2. (1) Retrieve — Does the model need external info? (2) ISREL — Is the retrieved data relevant? (3) ISSUP — Is the output supported by retrieved data? (4) ISUSE — Is the final output useful?

**Q3. How does ISSUP function as a fact-checker?** A3. ISSUP measures how well the LLM's generated text is supported by the retrieved information. If the generated claim contradicts or lacks support in the retrieved context, the ISSUP score will be low, signaling the model to revise.

**Q4. What is "Flexible Inference" in Self-RAG?** A4. It is the ability to adjust the weights assigned to reflection tokens at inference time (not during training), allowing users to tune the model toward precision or creativity without retraining the entire model.

**Q5. What type of LLM does Self-RAG require, and why?** A5. Self-RAG requires a specifically **fine-tuned** LLM (e.g., Llama-2, Mistral) trained to generate the special reflection tokens. Generic LLMs without this fine-tuning cannot produce the reflection tokens needed for the self-assessment mechanism.

# 12. C-RAG vs SELF-RAG vs FLARE — COMPARISON

|Feature|C-RAG|Self-RAG|FLARE|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Primary Focus**|Evaluate quality of _retrieved documents_ before generation|Decide _when_ to retrieve AND _how to use_ it via internal reflection|Decide _when_ to retrieve based on model confidence _during generation_|
|**Mechanism**|External retrieval evaluator assigns confidence scores; triggers web search fallback|Reflection tokens (Retrieve, ISREL, ISSUP, ISUSE) baked into model vocabulary|Token probability thresholds during generation trigger active retrieval|
|**When Retrieval is Triggered**|If evaluator finds documents irrelevant or ambiguous|When the model generates a Retrieve token|When any token in generation has low probability|
|**What Retrieval Uses**|Standard vector/keyword retrieval|Standard retrieval triggered by model decision|High-confidence generated tokens used as the query (forward-looking)|
|**Fallback**|Web search|N/A (re-evaluates via reflection)|Re-generates with retrieved context|
|**LLM Requirement**|Any instruction-following LLM (plug-and-play)|Requires a specifically fine-tuned LLM trained to produce reflection tokens|Works with many LLMs that can provide log probabilities (confidence scores)|
|**Primary Use Case**|Messy, irrelevant, or outdated retrieval sources|Applications requiring verified, accurate, self-assessed outputs|Long-form generation requiring proactive, confidence-triggered retrieval|

**Key One-Liners:**

- **C-RAG**: "Is what I retrieved any good? If not, search the web."
- **FLARE**: "Am I confident in what I'm about to say? If not, go look it up first."
- **Self-RAG**: "Do I need to retrieve? If yes, is it relevant? Is my answer supported? Is it useful?"

Both C-RAG and Self-RAG can be used together in high-performance systems like **LangGraph**.

---

### Q&A — Section 12: Comparison

**Q1. What is the fundamental distinction between C-RAG's and Self-RAG's retrieval triggers?** A1. C-RAG uses an external evaluator to assess the quality of already-retrieved documents and triggers retrieval fallback (web search) if they are poor. Self-RAG uses internal reflection tokens to decide from scratch whether retrieval is needed and then self-evaluates the retrieved content.

**Q2. Why can C-RAG work with any LLM while Self-RAG requires a fine-tuned LLM?** A2. C-RAG's evaluation logic is external to the LLM — any capable LLM can be plugged in as the generator. Self-RAG requires the LLM itself to produce reflection tokens, which requires specific fine-tuning on a custom vocabulary.

**Q3. In what way is FLARE distinct from both C-RAG and Self-RAG?** A3. FLARE is unique in being forward-looking and confidence-triggered _during_ the generation process. It uses token-level probability scores to detect uncertainty mid-generation and retrieves what's needed for upcoming sentences, rather than evaluating past retrieval or deciding before generation.

# 13. AGENTIC RAG

## Why Agents? — Challenges of Standard RAG

Standard RAG systems struggle with:

- **Complex reasoning** and multi-step processes
- **Searching through multiple data sources** simultaneously (highly inefficient without intelligent routing)
- Lack of **dynamic decision-making** during retrieval and generation

## Definition

**Agentic RAG** is a superior version of Standard RAG that uses **one or more LLMs as autonomous agents** to make dynamic decisions throughout the RAG process. Wherever dynamic decision-making can improve the system, an agent makes those decisions autonomously.

## Traditional vs Agentic RAG

|Dimension|Traditional RAG|Agentic RAG|
|---|---|---|
|Prompt Engineering|Heavy reliance on manual, static prompting|Dynamically modifies prompts based on situation and goal|
|Contextual Information|Relatively little; passive decision-making|Rich contextual understanding; active decision-making|
|Efficiency|Wasteful retrievals and general text generation|Chooses right time and place for retrieval, evaluates data quality, verifies answers|
|Complexity|Limited to simple single-hop queries|Handles multi-step, multi-hop, multi-source queries via tool use|

## 4 Components of Agentic RAG (Autonomous Decision Making)

### 1. Core LLM Brain

- The central LLM assigned a specific **role and task**.
- Example: A travel assistant agent handles searching, creating itineraries, booking tickets, managing previous bookings.

### 2. Memory

- Manages the agent's past experiences.
- **Short-term memory**: Chat history of the current conversation (current context).
- **Long-term memory**: Important info from previous interactions (e.g., ticket booking history, past travel searches).

### 3. Planning

Creates a step-by-step sequence of tasks. Three sub-components:

- **Task Decomposition**: Breaking complex tasks into smaller, manageable sub-tasks.
- **ReAct (Reasoning + Acting)**: The reasoning-action loop (covered in Section 14).
- **Reflection**: The agent does a self-assessment of outcomes.

### 4. Tools

- Assist the agent to perform **actions on external resources**.
- Examples: web search, querying an external API, weather API calls, database queries.
- The Core LLM Brain sends structured payloads to tools in an accepted format.

## Implementing Agentic RAG

### LlamaIndex

- Robust framework for agentic systems.
- Pre-built tools for different data sources.
- **Chains** feature enables chaining multiple tools and LLMs together.
- Dedicated toolkits for chatbots and question-answering systems.

### LangChain

- Full-featured toolkit for agent-based systems.
- Native integration of external resources.
- **Composability**: Mix and match query engines and data structures.

---

### Q&A — Section 13: Agentic RAG

**Q1. What is the core limitation of standard RAG that Agentic RAG addresses?** A1. Standard RAG lacks dynamic decision-making. It cannot handle complex multi-step reasoning, efficiently route queries to appropriate data sources, or autonomously adjust its behavior based on intermediate results.

**Q2. List the four components of an Agentic RAG system.** A2. (1) Core LLM Brain — the central reasoning agent; (2) Memory — short-term (current session) and long-term (historical); (3) Planning — task decomposition, ReAct, reflection; (4) Tools — external APIs, search engines, databases.

**Q3. What is the distinction between short-term and long-term memory in Agentic RAG?** A3. Short-term memory holds the current conversation context (what was said in the current session). Long-term memory stores important information from previous interactions (e.g., a user's past travel history or preferences).

**Q4. How does Agentic RAG reduce the need for manual prompt engineering?** A4. The agent dynamically modifies its prompts based on the current situation, goal, and intermediate results — it doesn't need a human to carefully craft every prompt for every scenario.

# 14. REACT PROMPTING

## Background — Limitations of Chain-of-Thought

**Chain-of-Thought (CoT)** prompting (Wei et al., 2022) shows LLMs how to reason step by step. However, it has limitations:

- Cannot **access external information**
- Cannot **update its knowledge base**
- Can lead to inaccurate facts and error propagation

## What is ReAct?

**ReAct (Reasoning + Acting)** was developed by Yao et al. in 2022. It enables LLMs to:

- Create **reasoning traces** (Thoughts)
- Perform **task-specific actions** (Acts) simultaneously
- Formulate, monitor, and revise action strategies
- Effectively manage unexpected situations
- Interact with external entities (knowledge databases, APIs, environments)

## The ReAct Loop

```
Thought → Action → Observation → (loop back to Thought)
```

|Component|Description|
|---|---|
|**Thought**|The model reasons about the task: "What do I know? What do I need?"|
|**Action**|The model generates a structured command to use an external tool (e.g., `Search[query]`)|
|**Observation**|The system intercepts the action, calls the external tool/API, and returns the result as an "Observation" fed back into the prompt|
|**Refinement**|The model uses the new info to decide next steps or produce the final answer|

> ReAct bridges the gap between **static internal knowledge** and **dynamic, real-world data**.

## ReAct Example — HotPotQA (Multi-hop Question)

**Question**: Which hotel hosts the Cirque du Soleil show "Mystère" and how many rooms does it have?

- **Thought**: "I need to find which hotel hosts 'Mystère' first."
- **Action**: Search for "Cirque du Soleil Mystère show"
- **Observation**: "The 'Mystère' show is hosted at the Treasure Island Hotel and Casino in Las Vegas."
- **Thought**: "Now I know the hotel. I need to find its room count."
- **Action**: Search for "Treasure Island Hotel and Casino number of rooms"
- **Observation**: "Treasure Island Hotel and Casino has 2,884 rooms."
- **Thought**: "I have both pieces of information."
- **Final Answer**: "The Treasure Island Hotel and Casino hosts the show 'Mystère' and has 2,884 rooms."

## Where is ReAct Used?

- **Primary home**: Agentic RAG — breaking down complex queries, formulating plans, using external tools iteratively.
- **Also used in**: Recursive RAG — while recursive retrieval defines _how_ to search, ReAct defines the _reasoning loop_ that triggers that recursion.

## LLM Requirements for ReAct

ReAct does NOT require a specific exclusive LLM, but the LLM must have:

|Requirement|Why Needed|
|---|---|
|Strong Reasoning/Logic|Must break down problems into steps|
|Instruction Following|Must adhere strictly to Thought → Action → Observation format|
|Context Management|Must maintain state of conversation and previous actions|

Advanced models (GPT-4, Claude 3) excel at this, but effectiveness depends more on the **structured prompt and tool integration** than the specific model.

**Implementation**: Few-shot prompts demonstrating the Thought-Action-Observation cycle + libraries like LangChain to bridge the model with external tools + a mechanism to parse model output and execute actions.

---

### Q&A — Section 14: ReAct Prompting

**Q1. What does ReAct stand for and who introduced it?** A1. ReAct stands for Reasoning + Acting. It was developed by Yao et al. in 2022.

**Q2. What are the three components of the ReAct loop?** A2. Thought (reasoning about the task), Action (structured command to use an external tool), and Observation (result returned from the external tool, fed back to the model).

**Q3. What key limitation of Chain-of-Thought prompting does ReAct overcome?** A3. CoT cannot access external information or update its knowledge base. ReAct enables the LLM to interact with external tools (search engines, APIs, databases) to get real-world data during the reasoning process.

**Q4. Is ReAct only for Agentic RAG?** A4. No. While the primary home is Agentic RAG, ReAct can also be applied within Recursive RAG systems to define the reasoning loop that triggers recursive search steps.

**Q5. Can any LLM do ReAct prompting? What capabilities are required?** A5. Not just any LLM — the model needs strong reasoning/logic, strict instruction following (Thought → Action → Observation format), and context management to track conversation state. Advanced models like GPT-4 and Claude 3 are best suited.

# 15. RAG INDEXING PIPELINE (IN DEPTH)

The **Indexing Pipeline** prepares the external knowledge base for retrieval. It has four main components.

## Overview

```
Raw Data → Data Loading → Data Splitting (Chunking) → Data Conversion (Embedding) → Data Storage (Vector DB)
```

## 15.1 Data Loading

Breaking down into sub-steps:

1. **Connect to the data source** (file system, database, API, URL, etc.)
2. **Extract text** from the file (PDF, HTML, DOCX, etc.)
3. **Review and update metadata**:
    - Metadata = information _about_ the data: type, origin, purpose, author, creation date, size, quality
    - Metadata is critical at retrieval time for filtering
    - Can resolve conflicting information by checking chronology or origin
    - Must be manually added for many data sources
4. **Clean and transform the data** (remove formatting artifacts, normalize text)

## 15.2 Data Splitting (Chunking)

**Definition**: Breaking down long pieces of text into manageable sizes.

### Why Chunk?

- LLMs have limited context windows
- LLM accuracy degrades when required content is **in the middle** of a long prompt (the "lost in the middle" problem)
- Retrievers find it easier to search over smaller pieces of text

### The Chunking Process (Three-Step)

1. Divide longer text into compact, meaningful units (sentences or paragraphs)
2. Merge smaller units into larger chunks until a target size is reached — treat as an independent segment
3. When creating a new chunk, **include an overlap** from the end of the previous chunk → maintains **contextual continuity**

### Chunking Methods

|Method|Description|Use Case|
|---|---|---|
|**Split by Character**|Split using a specific character (e.g., `\n` or `*`); chunk size measured in characters; overlap also specified in characters. See `CharacterTextSplitter` in LangChain|Simple text|
|**Recursively Split by Character**|Specify a _list_ of characters; tries first character, falls back to next if chunk size target not met. **Recommended for generic texts**. See `RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter`|Generic text (recommended default)|
|**Split by Token**|Splitting happens on a character boundary, but chunk _size and overlap_ are measured in **tokens** (since LLMs process tokens). More aligned with LLM processing|LLM-aware chunking|
|**Specialized Chunking**|Structure-aware splitting for HTML, Markdown, JSON, code. See `MarkdownHeaderTextSplitter`, `HTMLHeaderTextSplitter` in LangChain|Structured formats|
|**Adaptive Chunking**|Works well for structured data like customer reviews or patient records where each record = one chunk, regardless of length|Variable-length structured records|
|**Semantic Chunking**|Groups three sentences, then merges groups with similar _meaning_ using embeddings. Still experimental. See `SemanticChunker` in `langchain_experimental.text_splitter`|Meaning-based grouping|

### Large vs Small Chunks Trade-Off

|Small Chunks|Large Chunks|
|---|---|
|Precise, focused information → better retrieval accuracy|More context, coherence, overall themes preserved|
|May lose broader context and document themes|May introduce noise and irrelevant information|
|Better for targeted, factual queries|Better for holistic, thematic queries|

## 15.3 Data Conversion (Embedding)

Chunks of text are converted into **vector embeddings** (high-dimensional numerical representations) to enable semantic similarity search.

### Key Considerations

- **MTEB Leaderboard**: Benchmark for embedding models across 7 tasks — Classification, Clustering, Pair Classification, Reranking, Retrieval, Semantic Text Similarity, Summarization.
- **Cost** is the other major factor.

### Popular Embedding Models

|Provider|Models|
|---|---|
|OpenAI|`text-embedding-ada-002`, `text-embedding-3-small`, `text-embedding-3-large`|
|Google (Gemini)|`text-embedding-004` (updated April 2024)|
|Anthropic (Voyage AI)|`voyage-large-2-instruct`, `voyage-law-2`, `voyage-code-2`, `voyage-large-2`|
|Mistral AI|Mistral Embedding|
|Cohere|Cohere Embeddings|

## 15.4 Data Storage (Vector Database)

Vector databases store high-dimensional embeddings and support fast **similarity search**.

### Key Features of Vector Databases

- Specialize in indexing and storing vector embeddings for fast **semantic search**
- Offer similarity search based on **Euclidean Distance** or **Cosine Similarity**
- Employ specialized indexing techniques
- Provide traditional DB features: scalability, security, multi-tenancy, versioning

### Categories of Vector Storage

|Category|Examples|
|---|---|
|**Vector Indices** (bare-bones, search/indexing focused)|FAISS (Facebook AI Similarity Search), NMSLIB, ANNOY, ScaNN|
|**Specialized Vector Databases** (full data management)|Pinecone, ChromaDB, Milvus, Qdrant, Weaviate, Vald, LanceDB, Vespa, Marqo|
|**Traditional SQL with Vector**|Azure SQL, PostgreSQL|
|**NoSQL with Vector**|MongoDB|
|**Graph DB with Vector**|Neo4j|

### Choosing a Vector Database — Key Trade-offs

|Factor|Consideration|
|---|---|
|Accuracy vs Speed|More accurate algorithms may be slower; balance based on application needs|
|Flexibility vs Performance|More customizations add overhead and slow systems|
|Local vs Cloud Storage|Edge/privacy needs vs scalability|
|Direct Access vs API|Latency vs ease of integration|
|Simplicity vs Advanced Features|Development speed vs feature richness|
|Cost|Total cost of ownership|

### Q&A — Section 15: Indexing Pipeline

**Q1. What are the four main components of the RAG Indexing Pipeline?** A1. Data Loading, Data Splitting (Chunking), Data Conversion (Embedding), and Data Storage (Vector Database).

**Q2. Why is overlap important when chunking text?** A2. Overlap ensures that context from the end of one chunk is repeated at the beginning of the next, maintaining contextual continuity across chunk boundaries.

**Q3. What does the MTEB Leaderboard measure?** A3. It benchmarks embedding models across seven tasks: Classification, Clustering, Pair Classification, Reranking, Retrieval, Semantic Text Similarity, and Summarization.

**Q4. What is the key difference between Split by Character and Split by Token?** A4. Split by Character measures chunk sizes and overlap in characters. Split by Token measures them in tokens (the units LLMs actually process), making it more aligned with how the LLM will consume the content.

**Q5. What is Semantic Chunking and why is it "experimental"?** A5. Semantic Chunking groups sentences and merges groups that are similar in _meaning_ using embedding similarity. It's experimental because it's computationally more expensive and its effectiveness varies across domains.

**Q6. Name three types of similarity metrics or indexing methods used in vector databases.** A6. Euclidean Distance, Cosine Similarity, and specialized Approximate Nearest Neighbor (ANN) algorithms (used by FAISS, ANNOY, etc.).

# 16. RAG GENERATION PIPELINE (IN DEPTH)

The **Generation Pipeline** handles the actual Retrieval, Augmentation, and Generation at query time.

## 16.1 Retrieval

**Definition**: A search problem — find the documents that best match the input query.

### BM25 (Best Match 25)

BM25 is a classic **keyword-based** retrieval algorithm derived from probabilistic models.

**Components of BM25:**

**Term Frequency (TF) with Saturation:**

- Counts how often a term appears in a document.
- BM25 modifies TF with a **saturation factor**: additional occurrences of a word contribute _diminishing_ value (not linear). This prevents common words from dominating.
- BM25 also **length-normalizes** the TF calculation.

**Inverse Document Frequency (IDF):**

- Measures how rare a term is across the entire corpus.
- BM25 uses a refined probabilistic IDF formula.
- IDF does NOT require length normalization.

**Using BM25:**

- Index the document collection using BM25.
- When a query arrives, BM25 scores each document.
- Documents are **ranked by BM25 score** and the top-ranked ones retrieved.

### Popular Retrievers in LangChain

LangChain abstracts TF-IDF, embeddings/similarity search, and BM25 as ready-to-use retrievers. Developers can also build custom retrievers combining multiple methodologies.

## 16.2 Augmentation

**Definition**: Combining the user query with the retrieved information to create a prompt for the LLM.

- Augmentation falls under **prompt engineering**.
- The goal is to create a prompt that leads the LLM to accurate, relevant, grounded responses.

### Key Prompt Engineering Techniques for Augmentation

|Technique|Description|
|---|---|
|**Simple Contextual Prompting**|Add retrieved information to the prompt with instructions to focus on the provided context|
|**Controlled Generation Prompting**|Instruct the LLM to admit lack of knowledge when retrieved information is insufficient|
|**Few-Shot Prompting**|Provide examples of expected responses to guide format/style|
|**Chain of Thought (CoT) Prompting**|Add intermediate reasoning steps for complex tasks|
|**Self-Consistency, Tree of Thought, etc.**|Advanced techniques for complex reasoning (see Section 19)|

---

### Q&A — Section 16: Generation Pipeline

**Q1. What are the two key modifications BM25 makes to standard TF-IDF?** A1. (1) BM25 applies a saturation factor to TF, ensuring additional occurrences contribute diminishing returns (not linear). (2) BM25 uses a refined probabilistic IDF formula. BM25 also length-normalizes the TF calculation.

**Q2. What is "Augmentation" in RAG?** A2. Augmentation is the process of combining the user's query with the retrieved documents/chunks into a single, coherent prompt that the LLM then uses to generate a grounded answer.

**Q3. Why is "Controlled Generation Prompting" useful in RAG?** A3. Because RAG systems should not hallucinate when retrieved context is insufficient. Instructing the LLM to admit uncertainty ("I don't have enough information to answer this") prevents confident but wrong answers.

# 17. FINE-TUNING vs RAG

## Fine-Tuning Disadvantages

|Disadvantage|Explanation|
|---|---|
|**Catastrophic Forgetting**|Fine-tuned models often lose general capabilities from pre-training (e.g., a finance-fine-tuned LLM may no longer handle general conversation)|
|**Training Data Dependence**|Performance is entirely dependent on the quantity and quality of available domain training data; collecting good data is expensive|
|**Lacks External Knowledge**|Model only knows what was in its training data; no access to real-world updates|
|**Not Customizable Without Retraining**|Any change requires expensive retraining|

## RAG Advantages

- Retains all capabilities from pre-training (LLM itself is not modified)
- Augments the LLM with customizable external knowledge sources
- Allows changing knowledge sources without retraining
- Lower data requirements

> RAG systems often achieve **better performance than fine-tuning** while retaining more of the original LLM's capabilities.

## Choosing Based on Model Size

### Large LLMs (GPT-4, hundreds of billions to trillions of parameters)

→ **RAG is generally preferable** over fine-tuning.

### Medium LLMs (Llama 2 7B, Falcon 7B, Alpaca 7B — hundreds of millions of parameters)

→ Both viable. Choose based on use case:

- **Fine-tuning preferred** if task heavily relies on memorization (e.g., document QA).
- **RAG preferred** for domain-specific generation or classification needing retrieved knowledge.

### Small Custom LLMs (Zephyr, Phi2, Orca — thousands to millions of parameters)

→ **Fine-tuning is more suitable**:

- Small models already lack general capabilities.
- Minimal risk of catastrophic forgetting (little pre-training to forget).
- Easy to retrain with new data.

## Case Studies

### Customer Service Chatbot → Both RAG + Fine-Tuning

- Fine-tune a small model on internal datasets for company-specific knowledge.
- Augment via RAG for general chitchat and dynamic FAQ retrieval.

### Investment Management → RAG Suits Better

- Augment GPT-4 with client profiles and market data.
- Retrieve real-time market data without retraining.
- Fine-tuning risks damaging general conversational abilities needed for client communication.

### Anti-Money Laundering (AML) Text Analysis → Fine-Tuning Suits Better

- Highly specialized, static domain (laws, regulations, suspicious patterns).
- Text classification from learned patterns — fine-tuning is optimal.
- General conversational skills are less important.

---

### Q&A — Section 17: Fine-Tuning vs RAG

**Q1. What is "catastrophic forgetting" and why is it a problem with fine-tuning?** A1. Catastrophic forgetting is when a fine-tuned model loses general capabilities from pre-training. For example, a model fine-tuned on finance data may no longer handle everyday conversational queries well.

**Q2. For what model size is fine-tuning generally preferred over RAG, and why?** A2. For small custom LLMs (thousands to millions of parameters) like Zephyr, Phi2, Orca. These models have minimal pre-training to forget, so fine-tuning carries little risk of catastrophic forgetting, and they're easy to retrain.

**Q3. Why does RAG generally outperform fine-tuning for very large LLMs?** A3. Very large LLMs have extensive, broad general capabilities from pre-training. Fine-tuning risks degrading these capabilities (catastrophic forgetting). RAG augments the model without modifying its weights, preserving all pre-trained capabilities while adding domain-specific knowledge dynamically.

**Q4. Why is the AML text analysis task better suited to fine-tuning than RAG?** A4. AML analysis relies on recognizing static, specialized patterns (regulations, suspicious activities). It is a classification task based on learned domain patterns, not dynamic retrieval. General conversational ability is less important, making fine-tuning the better fit.

# 18. MODULAR RAG

## Definition

**Modular RAG** is an architecture that breaks down the traditional monolithic RAG structure into **interchangeable, independent components**.

- Allows **tailoring** of the system to specific use cases.
- Core components (indexing, retrieval, augmentation, generation) become **flexible, plug-and-play modules**.
- Additional specialized modules can be added or removed.

## Core Modules

All the standard Advanced RAG components (pre-retrieval, retrieval, augmentation, generation) are composed as interchangeable modules.

## Specialized Modules

|Module|Description|
|---|---|
|**Search Module**|Performs search across multiple, heterogeneous data sources|
|**RAG-Fusion**|Expands the user query into multiple diverse perspectives using an LLM, then conducts parallel searches across all perspectives|
|**Routing**|Navigates query through diverse data sources, selecting the optimal pathway (summarization, specific DB search, information merge)|
|**Task Adapter**|Fine-tunes the RAG system for specific tasks (summarization, translation, sentiment analysis) using a small number of task-specific examples without extensive retraining|
|**Memory Module**|Leverages the LLM's inherent parametric knowledge; uses reflection tokens or prompts to decide when to retrieve vs use internal knowledge|

---

### Q&A — Section 18: Modular RAG

**Q1. What is the key architectural advantage of Modular RAG over traditional monolithic RAG?** A1. Components are interchangeable and independently replaceable, allowing the system to be tailored to specific use cases by mixing and matching modules without rebuilding the entire pipeline.

**Q2. What does the RAG-Fusion module do?** A2. It expands the original user query into multiple diverse perspectives using an LLM, then conducts parallel searches for each perspective — increasing the breadth and diversity of retrieved information.

**Q3. What is the Routing module responsible for?** A3. Routing navigates the query through diverse data sources and selects the optimal retrieval pathway for a given query — whether summarization, specific database search, or merging information streams.

# 19. PROMPTING TECHNIQUES IN RAG SYSTEMS

## 19.1 Zero-Shot Prompting

The model is given no examples — only a task description. Has a steeper learning curve for complex tasks. Even advanced models struggle with complex multi-step reasoning in zero-shot.

## 19.2 Few-Shot Prompting (K-Shot)

Provide k examples to guide the model's response. Key insight from the **GPT-3 paper** ("Language Models are Few-Shot Learners"): as the number of parameters goes up, k-shot performance improves significantly for all k.

## 19.3 Chain-of-Thought (CoT) Prompting

**Core Idea**: "Thinking out loud" — provide examples that show intermediate reasoning steps, not just the final answer. Encourages the LLM to decompose complex problems into intermediate steps.

```
Example:
Q: Roger has 5 balls. He buys 2 cans of 3 tennis balls each. How many total?
A (CoT): Two cans have 2×3=6 balls. Add to original 5: 5+6=11. Answer: 11.
```

## 19.4 CoT with Self-Consistency (CoT-SC)

**How it works:**

1. Prompt the same question multiple times using CoT.
2. Sample multiple different reasoning paths from the LLM's decoder.
3. Aggregate the most **consistent** (most frequent) answer as the final result.

- **Ensemble approach**: returns the majority answer.
- **Tradeoff**: Improves performance but is **n times slower** (n = number of samples).

## 19.5 Contrastive Chain-of-Thought

- Provides **both correct and wrong examples** to explicitly show the model what mistakes to avoid.
- Helpful when specific error types are known in advance.

**Example:**

- Correct: "2 cans × 3 balls = 6 balls; 5 + 6 = 11."
- Wrong: "5 + 2 = 7" (incorrectly adding number of cans instead of balls in cans).

## 19.6 Tree of Thought (ToT)

**Core Idea**: Instead of a single linear reasoning path, explore **multiple branches** at each step.

**Process:**

1. At each reasoning step, the model explores **different solution branches**.
2. The model **votes for the best branch** and continues to the next step.
3. Branches are **pruned** based on feasibility scores after each round.

**Key Property**: Leverages LLM randomness to generate diverse ideas + uses comprehension to evaluate quality.

**Practical Implementation (Without Multiple Model Calls)**:

- Instead of calling the model multiple times, ask it to **simulate a conversation between multiple experts**.
- Experts question each other until they reach consensus.
- This mimics ToT behavior in a single prompt.

**ToT Example — The Ball in the Cup:**

Problem: Bob carries a ball in a cup to the bedroom, turns the cup upside down, walks to the garden, puts the cup down.

- Expert 1 → Expert 2 → Expert 3 collaborate through steps, each refining their answer.
- Final consensus: "The ball is in the bedroom" (it fell out when the cup was turned upside down).

**Limitation**: Requires many model calls → significantly slower. The multi-expert simulation approach addresses this.

## 19.7 Thread of Thoughts (ThoT)

**Context**: LLMs struggle in **chaotic contexts** (many retrieved passages, distractors, disorganized information). CoT follows a linear path and struggles with chaotic, unstructured data.

**ThoT Principle**: Inspired by human cognitive processes — systematically analyze, segment, and select relevant information from chaotic context, maintaining continuity of ideas.

**Two-Tiered Prompting System:**

1. **First pass**: Analyze and segment the chaotic context, walking through it in manageable parts.
2. **Second pass**: Summarize and derive conclusions from the segmented analysis.

**Key Differences from CoT:**

|Feature|CoT|ThoT|
|---|---|---|
|Context type|Linear, structured|Chaotic, disorganized|
|Process|Single reasoning chain|Two-tiered: analyze → summarize|
|Adaptability|Less flexible|Adaptable to various LLMs and prompting techniques|

**ThoT demonstrated marked improvement** in reasoning performance on chaotic multi-passage retrieval tasks.

**Supported by**: Advanced LLMs capable of complex reasoning (GPT-4, GPT-3.5). Implemented via API or specialized frameworks like LangChain.

---

### Q&A — Section 19: Prompting Techniques

**Q1. What is Chain-of-Thought prompting and why does it help?** A1. CoT provides examples showing intermediate reasoning steps (not just final answers), encouraging the LLM to decompose complex problems step by step. It helps because directly translating complex semantics into a single answer is hard for LLMs.

**Q2. What are the three steps of CoT Self-Consistency?** A2. (1) Prompt the LLM with CoT multiple times for the same question. (2) Sample multiple diverse reasoning paths. (3) Aggregate and return the most consistent (most frequent) answer.

**Q3. What is the key cost of CoT-SC?** A3. It is n times slower where n is the number of output samples, since the same question must be answered multiple times.

**Q4. How does Tree of Thought differ from Chain of Thought?** A4. CoT generates a single linear reasoning chain. ToT explores multiple reasoning branches at each step, scores them for feasibility, prunes weak branches, and votes for the best path — enabling richer, multi-path reasoning.

**Q5. What problem does Thread of Thoughts (ThoT) specifically address?** A5. ThoT addresses the problem of chaotic contexts — situations where LLMs must reason over many retrieved passages that are disorganized, contain distractors, or lack clear structure. CoT's linear reasoning fails in such environments.

**Q6. What is the "Two-Tiered Prompting System" in ThoT?** A6. The first tier walks through the chaotic context in manageable parts, analyzing and segmenting it. The second tier summarizes and derives conclusions from the first-tier analysis.

# 20. KNOWLEDGE GRAPH RAG

## Motivation

Standard vector-based RAG fails for:

- **Multi-hop reasoning** (e.g., "Find all patients taking Drug X who also have Condition Y linked to Drug Z's side effects")
- Identifying **contextual relationships** between entities
- Queries requiring understanding of **how entities are interconnected**

**Knowledge Graph RAG** = **hybrid approach** combining vector-based retrieval + graph traversal. It is not a replacement for vector RAG but a complement.

## Knowledge Graph Terminology

|Term|Definition|
|---|---|
|**Nodes**|Represent entities (people, places, products, concepts)|
|**Edges**|Represent relationships between entities|
|**Attributes**|Properties of entities (nodes) and relationships (edges)|
|**Triplets**|Knowledge represented as Node–Edge–Node (e.g., "Customer A → purchased → Product X"). These are the building blocks of knowledge graphs.|
|**Ontology**|Defines the schema/structure: types of entities, relationships, and their properties|
|**Graph Embeddings**|Vector representations of nodes and edges that capture graph structure|
|**Graph Query Language**|SPARQL, Cypher — languages for retrieving info from graphs, finding patterns and connections|
|**Graph Traversal**|Navigating through nodes and edges to discover paths, patterns, and insights|

## Knowledge Graph RAG Use Cases

- **Personalized Treatment Plans**: Link drugs, treatments, and conditions in a network → identify interactions and multi-factor treatment recommendations.
- **Personalized Product Recommendations**: Capture the interconnected path customers follow across transactions, browsing history, and feedback for holistic analysis.
- **Contract Analysis**: Map relationships among contracts, parties, and compliance requirements → identify conflicts, dependencies, and compliance risks across interconnected legal documents.

## Vector Search Enhanced by Graph (Retrieval Process)

The retrieval process has an **additional step of graph traversal** on top of standard vector search:

**Step 1 — Vector Search**: Standard similarity search based on the user query; identifies an initial set of highly similar chunks.

**Step 2 — Graph Traversal**: Traverse the knowledge graph to fetch **related entities** around the entities found in Step 1. This enriches the result with contextually connected information — deeper context for multi-hop queries.

**Outcome**: Both similar chunks AND parent chunks of related entities are retrieved.

## Graph Communities and Community Summaries

- **Graph Communities**: Subsets of entities that are more densely connected to each other than to the rest of the graph.
- **Community Detection**: Algorithms like the **Louvain algorithm** detect communities.
- **Community Summaries**: After detection, an LLM generates summaries of the entities and relationships within each community.
- Retrieval can then use vector similarity on these community summaries.
- Particularly useful for queries about **broader themes** in the knowledge base.

## Indexing Pipeline for Knowledge Graph RAG

|Step|Details|
|---|---|
|Data Loading|Same as standard vector RAG|
|Data Chunking|Same as vector RAG; chunks are then passed to an LLM for entity/relationship extraction|
|Entity Relationship Attribute Extraction|Use an LLM to extract entities and relationships directly from chunks|
|Storage|Entities and relationships stored in a graph database (e.g., **Neo4j**, supported by LangChain)|
|Community Summary|Apply community detection (Louvain); generate summaries via LLM; store in vector DB. **Microsoft's GraphRAG** library handles this.|

## Generation Pipeline for Knowledge Graph RAG

**Retrieval**: Graph databases like Neo4j have vector indices (via the **Neo4j Vector Search Plugin**) enabling similarity search on node embeddings. Queries can use:

- A standard Cypher query template
- An LLM-generated Cypher query

**Augmentation**: The graph query response is processed to extract text, then augmented to the original user query (same as standard RAG afterward).

**Generation**: Same as standard vector RAG — the augmented prompt goes to the LLM.

### Q&A — Section 20: Knowledge Graph RAG

**Q1. What is a "triplet" in a knowledge graph?** A1. A triplet is a Node–Edge–Node structure representing a fact: (Entity A) → [Relationship] → (Entity B). Example: "Customer A" → "purchased" → "Product X."

**Q2. Why is standard vector RAG insufficient for multi-hop reasoning queries?** A2. Standard vector RAG retrieves text chunks based on similarity to the query but cannot follow entity relationships across multiple hops. For example, it cannot naturally answer "Find patients on Drug X who have a condition linked to Drug Z's side effects" because this requires traversing a chain of relationships.

**Q3. What is the two-step retrieval process in Knowledge Graph RAG?** A3. Step 1: Standard vector similarity search to find initial chunks. Step 2: Graph traversal to fetch related entities and their parent chunks surrounding the initial results, adding deeper contextual information.

**Q4. What are Graph Communities and how are they detected?** A4. Graph communities are subsets of entities that are more densely connected to each other than to the rest of the graph. They are detected using algorithms like the Louvain algorithm.

# 21. MULTIMODAL RAG

## Motivation

Real-world data is not just text. Documents can contain images, tables, charts, audio, and video. Multimodal RAG extends RAG to handle these non-text modalities.

## Use Cases

|Domain|Modalities Involved|
|---|---|
|Medical Diagnosis|Text (patient records), tables (lab results), images (X-rays, MRIs)|
|Investment Analysis|Text (commentary), charts (trends, projections), tables (balance sheets)|
|Buying Assistance|Product images, text descriptions, tabular specs, customer reviews|
|Coding Assistance|Code snippets, documentation text, function usage examples|
|Equipment Maintenance|Text (historical reports), images (visual inspection), sensor data (tabular), video feed|

## Indexing Pipeline — Data Loading

New data loaders needed for non-text modalities:

- **Pillow (PIL)**: Popular Python library for loading images
- **Unstructured**: Open-source library for ingesting various data formats (including images, PDFs, etc.)
- **Pydub**: Python library for loading audio files (WAV, MP3)
- **LangChain** integrates with the Unstructured library

## Indexing Pipeline — Chunking

|Modality|Chunking Method|
|---|---|
|Raw Audio|**Voice Activity Detection (VAD)**: chunk based on silences/background noise|
|Video|**Scene detection-based chunking**: identify major scene changes to segment|
|Tabular Data|All text chunking strategies apply (context enrichment, semantic chunking, etc.)|
|Images|Generally **NOT chunked**|
|Multi-modal general|`semantic_chunkers` library: intelligent chunking for text, video, and audio|

## Indexing Pipeline — Embedding and Storage

Three approaches to multimodal embedding:

### Approach 1: Shared/Joint Embedding Models

- Map diverse data types into a **unified embedding space**.
- Example: **Google Vertex AI** shared embeddings.
- Efficient for general understanding; may fall short for granular multimodal understanding.

### Approach 2: Modality-Specific Embeddings

- Separate embedding spaces for different modality pairs.
- **CLIP** (Contrastive Language-Image Pretraining): image-text embedding space.
- **CLAP** (Contrastive Language-Audio Pretraining): audio-text embedding space.
- Text, image, and audio embeddings stored **separately** in different spaces.
- Better granularity, more complex to manage.

### Approach 3: Convert Non-Text to Text

- Use a **multimodal LLM** to convert images/audio to text descriptions first.
- Then follow standard text-only RAG.
- **Tradeoff**: Simple pipeline, but **information loss is inevitable** (image details lost in text description).

**Storage**: Embeddings → vector database; raw media files → document store (e.g., **Redis**).

## Generation Pipeline

**Retrieval**: Depends on the embedding strategy:

- Shared multimodal embeddings → single similarity search irrespective of modality
- Modality-specific embeddings → **multi-vector approach** (retrieve each modality separately by cosine similarity)
- Text-converted data → text-based retrieval

**Augmentation**: Same as text RAG, but the augmented prompt includes **raw multimodal file** alongside the text prompt.

**Generation**: Uses **Multimodal LLMs**:

- Proprietary: **OpenAI GPT-4o**, **GPT-4o mini**, **Google Gemini**
- Open-source: **Meta Llama 3.2**, **Mixtral AI's Pixtral**

### Q&A — Section 21: Multimodal RAG

**Q1. What are the three approaches to embedding in Multimodal RAG?** A1. (1) Shared/joint embedding models (all modalities in one unified space, e.g., Google Vertex AI). (2) Modality-specific embeddings (separate spaces per modality pair: CLIP for image-text, CLAP for audio-text). (3) Convert all non-text data to text using a multimodal LLM, then use standard text RAG (with information loss).

**Q2. What is the tradeoff of the "convert to text" approach in Multimodal RAG?** A2. It simplifies the pipeline (no special embedding models needed), but information loss is inevitable — visual details, spatial relationships, and audio characteristics that are difficult to express in text will be lost.

**Q3. What is Voice Activity Detection (VAD) used for in Multimodal RAG?** A3. VAD is used to chunk raw audio data by detecting silences and background noise, splitting the audio into meaningful segments at natural pause points.

**Q4. What library is used for image loading in Multimodal RAG?** A4. Pillow (also known as PIL — Python Imaging Library).

# 22. RAG EVALUATION METRICS

## Why Evaluate?

RAG systems can still fail in three ways:

1. **Retrieval failure**: Insufficient context retrieved or irrelevant context retrieved
2. **Lack of groundedness (faithfulness)**: Response is not supported by retrieved context (LLM uses its own biased parametric knowledge instead)
3. **Answer irrelevance**: Even with good retrieval and grounded generation, the response may still not address the user's actual query

## The RAG Triad (TruEra Framework)

The **RAG Triad** is a comprehensive evaluation framework with three core metrics forming a triangle:

```
                    (User) Query
                   /             \
    Answer Relevance             Context Relevance
                   \             /
                    (LLM) Answer ←→ (RAG) Context
                         Groundedness (Faithfulness)
```

|Metric|What It Measures|
|---|---|
|**Context Relevance**|Does the retrieved context align with the query?|
|**Groundedness / Faithfulness**|Is the LLM's answer supported by the retrieved context?|
|**Answer Relevance**|Does the final answer actually address the user's question?|

### Trade-offs in the RAG Triad

- High context relevance → usually leads to better groundedness (model has pertinent info)
- High groundedness ≠ high answer relevance (can reproduce context faithfully but miss the point of the question)
- Optimizing answer relevance without faithfulness → responses may seem appropriate but contain hallucinations

## RAGAS (Retrieval Augmented Generation Assessment)

**RAGAS** is an evaluation framework by **Exploding Gradients** that assesses RAG systems **without extensive human annotations** (uses LLMs to calculate metrics).

**Required inputs:**

- Question (user query input to RAG)
- Answer (generated by the RAG pipeline)
- Contexts (retrieved chunks)
- Ground Truths (correct reference answers)

**How Answer Relevance is Computed in RAGAS:**

- Generate a response for the query.
- Prompt the LLM to generate questions from the response (multiple times).
- Compute the **mean cosine similarity** between generated questions and the original question.
- Assumption: if the answer correctly addresses the original question, questions generated from it should match the original question.

**Other RAG Evaluation Frameworks**: ARES, TruLens, DeepEval, RAGChecker.

## 22.1 Context Relevance Metrics

### Accuracy

$$\text{Accuracy} = \frac{TP + TN}{TP + TN + FP + FN}$$

**Variables:**

- TP = True Positives (relevant documents correctly retrieved)
- TN = True Negatives (irrelevant documents correctly not retrieved)
- FP = False Positives (irrelevant documents incorrectly retrieved)
- FN = False Negatives (relevant documents missed)

**Limitation**: Not the primary retrieval metric. In large knowledge bases, most documents are irrelevant → artificially high accuracy. Does not consider ranking.

### Precision

$$\text{Precision} = \frac{TP}{TP + FP} = \frac{\text{Relevant Documents Retrieved}}{\text{Total Documents Retrieved}}$$

**Question it answers**: "Of all the documents retrieved, how many were actually relevant?"

**Interpretation**: Higher precision = retriever is mostly retrieving relevant documents.

### Precision@K

$$\text{Precision@K} = \frac{\text{Relevant documents in top K}}{K}$$

- Measures proportion of relevant documents in the **top K** results.
- Critical for RAG because only the top results are used for augmentation.
- Example: If RAG uses top 5 documents, use **Precision@5**.

### Context Precision@K (RAGAS)

RAGAS computes context precision considering ground-truth relevance at each of the top K positions.

### Recall

$$\text{Recall} = \frac{TP}{TP + FN} = \frac{\text{Relevant Documents Retrieved}}{\text{Total Relevant Documents}}$$

**Question it answers**: "Of all the relevant documents that exist, how many did the retriever find?"

**Note**: Requires prior knowledge of the total number of relevant documents — challenging in large knowledge bases.

### F1 Score

The **harmonic mean** of Precision and Recall:

$$F_1 = \frac{2 \cdot \text{Precision} \cdot \text{Recall}}{\text{Precision} + \text{Recall}}$$

**General F-measure (Weighted Harmonic Mean):**

$$F = \frac{(\beta^2 + 1) \cdot P \cdot R}{\beta^2 \cdot P + R}$$

**Variables:**

- P = Precision
- R = Recall
- β = weighting factor
    - β = 1 (balanced F1, equal weight to P and R)
    - β < 1 → emphasizes Precision
    - β > 1 → emphasizes Recall
    - α = 1/(1+β²) where α ∈ [0,1]

### Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR)

Measures the reciprocal of the rank of the **first relevant document** in the result list:

$$\text{MRR} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{1}{\text{rank}_i}$$

**Variables:**

- N = total number of queries
- rank_i = rank of the first relevant document for query i

**Strength**: Useful when you care about how quickly the system finds a relevant document.

**Limitation**: Only looks at the first relevant document; may not be useful when multiple relevant results are important.

### Precision-Recall Curve

The Precision-Recall curve illustrates the tradeoff:

- **Recall always increases** (or stays the same) as more documents are considered.
- Precision may **drop** when the next document is irrelevant (recall same, precision drops) or **rise** when the next document is relevant (both increase — "right shift").

### Mean Average Precision (MAP)

MAP provides a summary measure over multiple queries.

**Average Precision (for a single query):**

The average of precision values at each rank position where a relevant document appears.

$$\text{AvgPrec} = \frac{1}{m} \sum_{k=1}^{m} P(R_k)$$

Where m = total relevant documents, R_k = rank set up to the k-th relevant document.

**MAP Formula:**

$$\text{MAP}(Q) = \frac{1}{|Q|} \sum_{j=1}^{|Q|} \frac{1}{m_j} \sum_{k=1}^{m_j} \text{Precision}(R_{jk})$$

**Variables:**

- |Q| = total number of queries
- m_j = number of relevant documents for query j
- R_{jk} = ranked retrieval set for query j up to the k-th relevant document
- Each information need (query) is weighted equally

**Example Calculation:**

- Query 1: Relevant docs at ranks 1, 3, 5. AvgPrec = (1/1 + 2/3 + 3/5) / 3 = (1 + 0.67 + 0.5)/3 = 0.62 (approximated in slides as (1+0.67+0.5+0.44+0.5)/5 = 0.62)
- Query 2: Relevant docs at ranks 1, 3, 4 → AvgPrec = (0.5+0.4+0.43)/3 = 0.44
- **MAP = (0.62 + 0.44) / 2 = 0.53**

### Discounted Cumulative Gain (DCG)

DCG measures both **relevance** and **ranking order**.

**Relevance Score**: rel_i = graded relevance of the item at rank position i.

Typically: 0 = not relevant, 1 = moderately relevant, 2 = relevant, 3 = very relevant.

**DCG Formula:**

$$\text{DCG}@K = \sum_{i=1}^{K} \frac{\text{rel}_i}{\log_2(i+1)}$$

**Intuition**: Relevant items placed higher get more credit. The logarithmic discount means the difference between positions 1 and 2 is greater than between positions 2 and 3, and so on.

**Example:**

- Highly relevant item (rel=3) at position 1, highly relevant item (rel=3) at position 4:
    - DCG = 3/log₂(2) + 3/log₂(5) = 3/1 + 3/2.32 = 3 + 1.29 = 4.29

**Limitation**: Absolute DCG values depend on list length and relevance scale → hard to compare across different use cases.

### Normalized DCG (nDCG)

To overcome DCG's limitation, normalize by the **Ideal DCG (IDCG)**:

$$\text{nDCG}@K = \frac{\text{DCG}@K}{\text{IDCG}@K}$$

**Variables:**

- IDCG@K = DCG value if all items appeared in perfect relevance order (best possible ranking)
- nDCG is always between 0 and 1
- Allows comparison across different queries and use cases

**Q1. What are the three failure modes that RAG evaluation frameworks address?** A1. (1) Retrieval failure (insufficient or irrelevant context). (2) Lack of groundedness (answer not supported by retrieved context). (3) Answer irrelevance (answer doesn't address the actual query).

**Q2. Explain the RAG Triad and the three metrics it contains.** A2. The RAG Triad (TruEra) evaluates: Context Relevance (how well retrieved context matches the query), Groundedness/Faithfulness (how well the generated answer is supported by retrieved context), and Answer Relevance (how well the final answer addresses the user's question).

**Q3. Why is Precision@K more relevant to RAG than overall Precision?** A3. RAG systems use only the top K retrieved documents for augmentation. Precision@K focuses specifically on those top K results, making it directly relevant to the actual documents that will influence the LLM's generation.

**Q4. What are the variables in the F-measure formula and what does β control?** A4. P = Precision, R = Recall, β = weighting factor. β=1 gives balanced F1 (equal weight). β<1 emphasizes Precision. β>1 emphasizes Recall.

**Q6. What does IDCG represent in nDCG?** A6. IDCG (Ideal Discounted Cumulative Gain) represents the DCG score if all items were ranked in perfect order of relevance — the best possible ranking. Dividing DCG by IDCG normalizes the score to the [0,1] range.

**Q7. How is Answer Relevance computed in RAGAS?** A7. The LLM is prompted to generate questions from its own response multiple times. The mean cosine similarity between these generated questions and the original user question is computed. If the answer truly addresses the original question, questions derived from the answer should closely match the original question.

# 23. HALLUCINATION — TYPES, DETECTION, AND MITIGATION

## Faithfulness / Groundedness

**Faithfulness** (also called Groundedness) measures how well the generated response is supported by the retrieved context.

**Hallucination Rate** is the **inverse** of Faithfulness:

$$\text{Hallucination Rate} = \frac{\text{Claims in response NOT in retrieved context}}{\text{Total claims in response}}$$

## Types of Hallucination

|Type|Description|Severity|
|---|---|---|
|**Word-Level (Nitpicky Nerd)**|Small factual errors (e.g., wrong birth year for a well-known figure)|Low|
|**Sentence-Level (Overconfident Liar)**|Entire sentence fabricated with false information (e.g., "Penguins are the only birds capable of flight at sub-zero temperatures")|Medium — "overconfidence bias"|
|**Document-Level (Full-On Fantasist)**|Entire document or extended passage fabricated (e.g., fake academic paper with fake citations)|High|
|**Cross-Domain (Mischievous Mixer)**|Combines facts from different domains incorrectly (e.g., "The Battle of Hastings was won using Python scripts")|Depends on context|
|**Contextual (Chameleon)**|Contextually plausible but factually wrong (e.g., chatbot discussing tea suddenly claims it's popular among Martians)|Variable|

## Hallucination Detection Challenges

- **Word-Level errors** look plausible (e.g., a wrong year is still a year).
- **Sentence-Level fabrications** may fool non-experts.
- **Document-Level inventions** overwhelm with detail — like a convincing sci-fi story.

## Hallucination Detection Methods

|Method|Description|Limitation|
|---|---|---|
|**Prediction Probability (PP)**|Flags tokens with unusually low confidence scores as potential hallucinations|Assumes well-calibrated models|
|**LLM as Judge**|Model generates multiple outputs for the same input and checks for consistency; AI grades its own homework|May have self-consistency bias|
|**Internal State Analysis**|Analyzes LLM's attention patterns and embeddings to detect when the model "goes off-script"|Hard to implement; requires model internals access|
|**Fact Verification**|Cross-reference output with reliable databases or knowledge graphs|Slow and resource-heavy|
|**Sampling-Based Methods**|Generate multiple responses; select the most factual/consistent one|Computationally expensive|
|**Attention Matrix Analysis**|If attention wanders to irrelevant tokens, hallucination is likely|Requires internal access|
|**Human Evaluation**|Experts manually check for hallucinations|Expensive, not scalable|

## Layered Hallucination Detection

Think of it as **multi-level security screening** — each layer catches what others miss:

1. **Token-level screening**: Prediction Probability and Entropy analysis
2. **Contextual checks**: Attention and consistency analysis
3. **Cross-referencing**: External fact verification (the "no-fly list" check)

> Reference: "Detecting Hallucination using Semantic Entropy" (paper mentioned in slides)

---

### Q&A — Section 23: Hallucination

**Q1. What is the mathematical relationship between Faithfulness and Hallucination Rate?** A1. They are inverses. Hallucination Rate = proportion of generated claims NOT found in the retrieved context. Faithfulness = proportion of generated claims that ARE supported by retrieved context.

**Q2. Name the five types of hallucination and give the severity of each.** A2. (1) Word-Level — Low. (2) Sentence-Level — Medium. (3) Document-Level — High. (4) Cross-Domain — Depends. (5) Contextual — Variable.

**Q3. What is "overconfidence bias" in the context of hallucination?** A3. It refers to an LLM's tendency to generate false information with high apparent confidence (sentence-level hallucination), producing fabricated claims in a convincing, authoritative tone.

**Q4. What is the Layered Hallucination Detection approach?** A4. A three-tier approach: (1) Token-level screening via Prediction Probability/Entropy; (2) Contextual checks via attention and consistency analysis; (3) Cross-referencing with external knowledge databases or knowledge graphs.

**Q5. Why is LLM-as-Judge potentially problematic for hallucination detection?** A5. The AI is essentially grading its own output. If the LLM has a consistent bias or a consistent blind spot, it may fail to detect its own hallucinations — a form of self-consistency bias.
